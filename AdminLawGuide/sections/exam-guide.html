Self-Assessment Quizzes

Test your knowledge with these multiple-choice questions covering key administrative law concepts.

1. Which of the following is NOT a requirement for Article III standing?

Correct Answer: B. Final agency action

Final agency action is a requirement for reviewability under the APA, not for Article III standing. The three requirements for Article III standing are injury in fact, causation, and redressability.

2. After Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024), how should courts approach agency statutory interpretations?

Correct Answer: B. Determine the best reading of the statute using traditional tools of interpretation

In Loper Bright, the Supreme Court overturned Chevron deference. Courts now must determine the best reading of a statute using traditional tools of statutory interpretation, rather than deferring to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes.

3. When is an agency's decision "committed to agency discretion by law" and thus unreviewable?

Correct Answer: B. When there is "no law to apply" to guide judicial review

Under APA § 701(a)(2), agency action is "committed to agency discretion by law" when the statute provides no meaningful standard against which to judge the agency's exercise of discretion. This is known as the "no law to apply" test from Citizens to Preserve Overton Park v. Volpe.

Doctrinal Timelines

These timelines trace the evolution of key administrative law doctrines through important Supreme Court cases, helping you understand how the field has developed over time.

Presidential Control Timeline

Myers v. United States (1926)

President has unrestricted power to remove executive officers he has appointed with Senate confirmation.

Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935)

Congress can limit President's removal power over commissioners of independent agencies who perform quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial functions.

Wiener v. United States (1958)

President could not remove a member of the War Claims Commission despite no explicit removal restrictions in statute.

Morrison v. Olson (1988)

Upheld independent counsel statute with "good cause" removal restriction, focusing on whether restriction impermissibly interferes with President's duties.

Free Enterprise Fund v. PCAOB (2010)

Struck down dual for-cause removal restrictions as unconstitutionally limiting presidential control.

Seila Law v. CFPB (2020)

Struck down single-director structure with removal protection, distinguishing Humphrey's as applying only to multi-member commissions.

This timeline shows the evolution of presidential removal power doctrine, illustrating the pendulum swings between greater presidential control and agency independence throughout the Court's history.

Administrative Rulemaking Timeline

Administrative Procedure Act (1946)

Established the basic framework for agency rulemaking, including notice-and-comment procedures in § 553.

United States v. Florida East Coast Railway (1973)

Limited formal rulemaking requirements, holding that "hearing" language alone does not trigger §§ 556-557 procedures.

Vermont Yankee v. NRDC (1978)

Courts cannot impose procedural requirements beyond those in the APA.

Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. v. State Farm (1983)

Established "hard look" review of agency rulemaking under the arbitrary and capricious standard.

Long Island Care at Home v. Coke (2007)

Recognized the "logical outgrowth" doctrine, requiring final rules to be logically connected to proposed rules.

Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Association (2015)

Agencies can change interpretive rules without notice and comment, overturning the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine.

This timeline traces key developments in administrative rulemaking procedure, showing the evolution from the APA's enactment through significant judicial interpretations that have shaped modern rulemaking practices.

Memory Aids for Key Doctrines

Administrative law contains many complex doctrines with multi-factor tests. These memory aids will help you recall the essential elements of each doctrine quickly.

MARS: Major Questions Analysis

Remember the key factors in Major Questions Doctrine analysis with the acronym "MARS":

  • Major economic or political significance
  • Authorization clarity from Congress
  • Regulatory history of the agency
  • Statutory context and purpose

Example: In West Virginia v. EPA, the Court examined whether the Clean Air Act's general provision to regulate air pollutants clearly authorized the EPA's sweeping regulations of power plant emissions. The Court found the economic and political significance too great for such general statutory language.

PAPER: Arbitrary & Capricious Review

Remember the State Farm factors for "hard look" review with the acronym "PAPER":

  • Pertinent data examination
  • Articulated explanation
  • Problem aspects consideration
  • Evaluating alternatives
  • Reasoned change justification

Example: In Department of Homeland Security v. Regents of the University of California, the Court invalidated the rescission of DACA because DHS failed to consider important aspects of the problem, including reliance interests of DACA recipients.

CRISP: Standing Requirements

Remember the elements of standing with the acronym "CRISP":

  • Concrete injury
  • Real and particularized harm
  • Imminent or actual
  • Sourced from defendant (causation)
  • Possible to redress

Example: In Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, the Court rejected standing because plaintiffs' possible future visits to areas affected by challenged actions were too speculative to constitute an "imminent" injury.

FINAL: Finality Doctrine

Remember Bennett v. Spear's two-part test for final agency action with "FINAL":

  • Full conclusion of decision-making
  • Impact that is direct
  • Now affecting rights or obligations
  • Authority exercised with force of law
  • Legal consequences flowing from action

Example: In U.S. Army Corps of Engineers v. Hawkes Co., the Court found that a jurisdictional determination under the Clean Water Act was final agency action because it marked the consummation of the agency's decision-making process and had legal consequences.

Arbitrary & Capricious Review Factors

Under Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm, a court will find agency action arbitrary and capricious if the agency:

  1. Failed to examine relevant data
  2. Failed to articulate a satisfactory explanation
  3. Failed to consider an important aspect of the problem
  4. Offered an explanation that runs counter to the evidence
  5. Offered an explanation so implausible it can't be attributed to expertise
  6. Relied on factors Congress did not intend the agency to consider
  7. Changed course without acknowledging the change or giving good reasons (including reliance interests)
Judicial Review Prerequisites
A.C.T.I.O.N. Framework for Judicial Review

Remember all the prerequisites for judicial review with this acronym:

  • Agency action (identifiable)
  • Cause of action (APA or other)
  • Timely (statute of limitations, ripeness)
  • Interests injured (standing)
  • Other remedies exhausted
  • Not precluded (by statute or committed to agency discretion)

If any element is missing, the court cannot proceed to the merits of the case.

Practice Arena

Apply your knowledge and test your understanding with these practice scenarios. Work through these examples to strengthen your ability to analyze administrative law problems under exam conditions.

Scenario 1: Agency Authority

The Department of Transportation (DOT) issues a rule requiring all smartphone manufacturers to include a feature that automatically disables text messaging capabilities when a phone is detected moving faster than 10 mph. The DOT claims authority under a statute that authorizes it to "issue regulations to enhance highway safety." Several technology companies challenge the rule.

Question: What doctrine(s) would you analyze to determine if DOT has the authority to issue this rule?

Analysis:

  1. Major Questions Doctrine: This is likely a question of vast economic and political significance, as it would impact every smartphone manufacturer and user in the country. Under this doctrine, the court would look for clear congressional authorization for DOT to regulate smartphone functionality.
  2. Statutory Authority: Does the broad language "enhance highway safety" clearly authorize regulation of smartphone manufacturing, which is not traditionally within DOT's regulatory sphere?
  3. Nondelegation Concerns: Is "enhance highway safety" an intelligible principle sufficient to guide the agency's discretion for this type of regulation?

Given recent Supreme Court cases like West Virginia v. EPA, a court would likely find that Congress did not clearly authorize DOT to impose design requirements on smartphones, even if the purpose relates to highway safety.

Scenario 2: Arbitrary and Capricious Review

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issues a final rule reducing the permissible levels of a certain air pollutant from industrial sources. In its rulemaking, the EPA discusses the health benefits of the regulation but doesn't address industry concerns about technological feasibility or economic impacts raised during the comment period. An industry group challenges the rule as arbitrary and capricious.

Question: How would a court likely analyze this challenge under State Farm?

Analysis:

Under Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm, a court would likely find this rule arbitrary and capricious because:

  1. The EPA failed to consider an important aspect of the problem (technological feasibility and economic impacts)
  2. The agency did not respond to significant comments raised during the notice-and-comment period
  3. The agency's explanation is incomplete, as it addresses only the benefits without considering the costs or implementation challenges

The court would likely remand to the agency to address these deficiencies in the rulemaking process, requiring the EPA to consider and respond to the industry concerns before finalizing the rule.

Hypothetical 1: Major Questions Doctrine

Facts: The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issues a rule requiring all social media platforms with more than 10 million users to implement content moderation policies that remove false or misleading information within 24 hours. The FCC relies on a provision in its organic statute authorizing it to "make such rules and regulations... as may be necessary in the execution of its functions." Several tech companies challenge the rule.

Questions:

  1. Would the Major Questions Doctrine apply to this agency action? Why or why not?
  2. How would the factors from West Virginia v. EPA apply?
  3. Would your analysis change if Congress had specifically authorized the FCC to "regulate social media platforms to prevent the spread of misinformation"?

Hypothetical 2: Standing

Facts: The Department of the Interior (DOI) lifts a ban on oil and gas leasing in a national wildlife refuge in Alaska. An environmental group whose members visit the refuge occasionally for bird-watching challenges the decision. The group argues that oil and gas development will harm wildlife and reduce their members' enjoyment of the area. DOI argues that the plaintiffs lack standing because development hasn't started yet and may never affect the specific areas the members visit.

Questions:

  1. Do the environmental group's members have standing under Lujan? Why or why not?
  2. How might the plaintiffs strengthen their standing arguments?
  3. Would the analysis change if the plaintiff was the State of Alaska rather than an environmental group?

Step-by-Step Analysis Models

These step-by-step analyses demonstrate the methodical approach necessary for administrative law problems. Each example walks through a structured analysis to help you develop this critical skill.

Judicial Review Analysis Model

The Administrative Rulemaking Authority (ARA) issues a rule requiring all online retailers to implement enhanced cybersecurity protocols. The rule will cost e-commerce companies approximately $2 billion annually to implement. The ARA cites its authority to "protect consumer information" under its enabling statute. An association of small online businesses challenges the rule.

Step 1: Threshold Issues (Access to Judicial Review)
  1. Reviewability:
    • Is judicial review precluded by statute? No explicit preclusion in the hypothetical.
    • Is the action committed to agency discretion by law? Likely not, as this is a substantive rule with clear standards.
  2. Final Agency Action:
    • Bennett v. Spear test: (1) Consummation of decision-making process? Yes, the rule is final. (2) Legal consequences flow? Yes, it imposes compliance obligations.
  3. Standing:
    • Injury in fact: Economic cost of compliance ($2 billion industry-wide) is concrete and particularized.
    • Causation: The injury is directly caused by the ARA's rule.
    • Redressability: A favorable court decision invalidating the rule would remedy the injury.
    • Zone of interests: Small businesses are regulated entities within the zone of interests of the statute.
  4. Ripeness: The rule is final, so the issue is fit for review, and withholding review would cause hardship (compliance costs).

Conclusion: Threshold requirements are satisfied. A court would likely proceed to the merits.

Step 2: Statutory Authority Analysis
  1. Major Questions Doctrine:
    • Question of vast economic significance? Yes, $2 billion annual cost is significant.
    • Clear congressional authorization? The general authority to "protect consumer information" may be too vague for such a costly and expansive regulation.
    • After West Virginia v. EPA, a court would likely require clearer authorization for this major regulatory action.
  2. Traditional Statutory Interpretation:
    • Text: What does "protect consumer information" encompass? Does it clearly include mandating specific cybersecurity protocols?
    • Purpose: Is the rule consistent with the consumer protection purpose of the statute?
    • Post-Loper Bright analysis: Without Chevron deference, is this the best reading of the statute?

Conclusion: The rule likely exceeds the agency's statutory authority under the Major Questions Doctrine, as "protect consumer information" is too general to authorize a $2 billion regulatory scheme.

Step 3: Arbitrary and Capricious Review
  1. State Farm Analysis: (If we reached this step)

State Farm Arbitrary & Capricious Analysis

The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issues a rule requiring graphic warning labels covering 80% of cigarette packaging. The FDA justifies the rule based on studies showing that larger, graphic warnings increase awareness of smoking risks. The FDA acknowledges but dismisses industry comments requesting a smaller coverage requirement of 50%, stating only that "80% is more effective." The FDA did not analyze the economic impact of the 80% requirement versus alternative percentages.

Apply the State Farm "hard look" factors to this scenario:

State Farm "Hard Look" Analysis:
  1. Examination of Relevant Data:
    • The FDA did examine studies on warning effectiveness, which is relevant data.
    • However, the FDA failed to analyze the economic impact of the 80% requirement versus alternatives, which is also relevant data.
    • A court would likely find this factor partially satisfied, but with significant deficiencies.
  2. Satisfactory Explanation:
    • The FDA's explanation that "80% is more effective" is conclusory and lacks substantive reasoning.
    • It fails to articulate why the marginal benefit of 80% versus 50% justifies the additional economic burden.
    • A court would likely find this explanation inadequate under State Farm.
  3. Important Aspects of the Problem:
    • The FDA failed to consider the economic impact, which is an important aspect of the problem.
    • The agency did not address possible First Amendment commercial speech implications of requiring 80% of packaging for warnings.
    • A court would likely find that the FDA failed to consider important aspects of the problem.
  4. Reasonable Alternatives:
    • The FDA dismissed the 50% alternative without substantive analysis.
    • The agency failed to explain why 80% was chosen over other possible percentages (60%, 70%, etc.).
    • A court would likely find that the FDA failed to adequately consider reasonable alternatives.

Conclusion: A court would likely find the rule arbitrary and capricious under the State Farm standard. The FDA's cursory dismissal of alternatives, failure to consider economic impacts, and inadequate explanation for the 80% requirement fall short of the reasoned analysis required by the APA. The court would likely remand to the agency for a more thorough analysis and explanation.

Standing Analysis Application

The Department of the Interior (DOI) grants permits for oil drilling in a national wildlife refuge. A conservation organization whose members occasionally visit the refuge for bird watching and photography challenges the permits. The organization's members claim they will suffer aesthetic and recreational injuries if drilling proceeds, though none have concrete plans to visit in the next year. The closest drilling site would be 20 miles from public access areas.

Apply the requirements for Article III standing to this scenario:

Standing Analysis:
  1. Injury in Fact:
    • The claimed injuries are aesthetic and recreational interests in observing wildlife.
    • Such interests can qualify as cognizable injuries under Lujan and Sierra Club v. Morton.
    • However, the lack of concrete plans to visit the refuge in the next year makes the injury speculative rather than "actual or imminent" as required by Lujan.
    • The distance of 20 miles between drilling sites and public access areas further weakens the concreteness and particularization of the injury.
    • Under Summers v. Earth Island Institute, a mere "someday" intention to visit is insufficient.
  2. Causation:
    • The causal connection between drilling 20 miles away and harm to bird watching opportunities is attenuated.
    • The organization would need scientific evidence showing how drilling would impact wildlife throughout the refuge.
    • Without such evidence, causation may be too speculative to satisfy Article III requirements.
  3. Redressability:
    • If permits were invalidated, drilling would not occur, which would redress the alleged injury.
    • This element is likely satisfied.
  4. Organizational Standing:
    • Under Hunt v. Washington State Apple Advertising Commission, an organization needs at least one member with standing to sue in their own right.
    • Given the weaknesses in individual standing, organizational standing is problematic.
  5. Zone of Interests:
    • Environmental and recreational interests are generally within the zone of interests of statutes governing national wildlife refuges.
    • This prudential standing requirement would likely be satisfied.

Conclusion: A court would likely find that the organization lacks standing primarily due to insufficient injury in fact. Without members who have concrete plans to visit the refuge, the injury remains too speculative and fails the "actual or imminent" requirement under Lujan. The organization could potentially establish standing by identifying members with specific, planned visits to the refuge or by gathering scientific evidence showing how drilling 20 miles away would concretely impact the entire ecosystem visitors experience.

Question: Is the USDA document likely to be classified as a legislative rule or an interpretive rule/policy statement? Explain your analysis.

Analysis:

This problem tests basic understanding of the distinction between legislative rules and interpretive rules/policy statements:

  1. Legislative Rule Indicators:
    • The document uses binding language ("must" and "must not") rather than suggestive language.
    • It appears to establish substantive requirements for organic certification, not merely explain existing requirements.
    • The title "Guidance" is not determinative; courts look to actual content and effect.
  2. Interpretive Rule Analysis:
    • An interpretive rule explains existing legal obligations without creating new ones.
    • If the pesticide requirements already exist in statutes or regulations, the document could be interpretive.
    • However, the problem suggests these are new requirements, not interpretations of existing ones.
  3. Policy Statement Analysis:

Intermediate Level: Statutory Authority Analysis

The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issues a rule requiring all internet service providers (ISPs) to provide a minimum of 100 Mbps download speeds to all customers by 2026. The rule is estimated to cost the industry $50 billion in infrastructure upgrades. The FCC cites its authority under the Communications Act to "make available... a rapid, efficient, nationwide... communication service with adequate facilities at reasonable charges." ISPs challenge the rule, arguing the FCC lacks statutory authority for such a sweeping mandate.

Question: Does the FCC have statutory authority for this rule? Apply relevant doctrines and cases in your analysis.

Analysis:

This intermediate problem requires applying both the Major Questions Doctrine and traditional statutory interpretation:

  1. Major Questions Doctrine:
    • The rule has vast economic significance ($50 billion cost to industry).
    • It represents a major transformation of the internet service market.
    • Under West Virginia v. EPA, courts expect clear congressional authorization for such significant regulatory actions.
    • The general language about "rapid, efficient, nationwide... communication service" lacks the specificity typically required for major questions.
  2. Traditional Statutory Interpretation (Post-Loper Bright):
    • Without Chevron deference, the court must determine the best reading of the statute using traditional tools of interpretation.
    • Text: Does "rapid, efficient" communication service authorize mandating specific speed minimums?
    • The term "rapid" is relative and subjective, not clearly authorizing a specific numerical requirement.
    • Context: The phrase "with adequate facilities at reasonable charges" suggests a balance between quality and cost, not a singular focus on speed.
    • Purpose: While promoting better communication services is a purpose of the Act, the statute also aims to maintain reasonable costs.
  3. Federalism Concerns:
    • The rule potentially intrudes on areas traditionally regulated by states.
    • Under Gregory v. Ashcroft, courts expect clear statements from Congress before interpreting statutes to alter federal-state balance.

Conclusion: The FCC likely lacks statutory authority for this specific rule. Under the Major Questions Doctrine, the general language about "rapid, efficient" service is insufficient to authorize a $50 billion nationwide speed mandate. Post-Loper Bright, without Chevron deference, the best reading of the statute does not support such a specific numerical requirement. A more tailored approach with flexible standards or one that accounts for regional differences would have a stronger statutory basis.

Advanced Level: Multi-Issue Analysis

The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) issues a rule requiring all publicly traded companies to disclose detailed information about their climate change risks and greenhouse gas emissions, including those of their suppliers and customers. The rule is estimated to cost companies $3.9 billion annually. The SEC Chair who approved the rule was appointed by the President after the Senate failed to act on the nomination for 120 days, using a provision in the SEC's organic statute that allows the President to appoint commissioners if the Senate does not act within 90 days. The rule was issued after notice and comment, but the final rule contains several significant provisions not included in the proposed rule. A coalition of business groups challenges the rule on multiple grounds.

Question: Analyze all plausible administrative law challenges to this rule. Which is most likely to succeed?

Multi-Issue Analysis:

This advanced problem requires analyzing multiple administrative law issues:

  1. Appointment Clause Challenge:
    • The SEC Chair was appointed without Senate confirmation using a statutory provision.
    • Under the Constitution's Appointments Clause (Art. II, § 2, Cl. 2), principal officers (like agency heads) require Senate confirmation.
    • Issue: Can a statute override the Constitution's appointment requirements?
    • Analysis: Under NLRB v. SW General and similar cases, a statutory appointment mechanism that bypasses Senate confirmation for a principal officer likely violates the Appointments Clause.
    • If the Chair's appointment was unconstitutional, actions taken by the Chair may be invalid.
  2. Major Questions Doctrine:
    • The rule has vast economic significance ($3.9 billion annual cost).
    • Climate disclosures represent a major policy decision with political and economic significance.
    • Issue: Does the SEC's general authority to require disclosures clearly authorize climate change and emissions disclosures?
    • Analysis: Under West Virginia v. EPA, courts expect clear congressional authorization for such significant regulatory actions. The SEC's general disclosure authority would likely be deemed insufficient for this specific requirement.
  3. Logical Outgrowth Doctrine:
    • The final rule contains significant provisions not in the proposed rule.
    • Issue: Were interested parties reasonably on notice of the potential changes?
    • Analysis: Under CSX Transportation v. Surface Transportation Board, a final rule must be a "logical outgrowth" of the proposed rule. Significant new provisions not anticipated from the proposal may violate this requirement.
  4. Statutory Authority:
    • Does the SEC's statutory authority to require disclosures "necessary or appropriate in the public interest or for the protection of investors" encompass climate risks and emissions?
    • Post-Loper Bright analysis: Without Chevron deference, is this the best reading of the statute using traditional tools of interpretation?
    • The link between some climate disclosures (especially Scope 3 emissions from customers) and investor protection may be tenuous.
  5. Arbitrary and Capricious Review:
    • Did the SEC examine relevant data on costs and benefits?
    • Did it consider important aspects like compliance difficulties for certain industries?
    • Did it adequately justify its decision to include Scope 3 emissions?

Most Likely to Succeed: The Major Questions Doctrine challenge is most likely to succeed. Given the $3.9 billion cost and the significant policy implications of mandatory climate disclosures, courts would expect clear congressional authorization. The SEC's general disclosure authority would likely be deemed insufficient for such a major economic and political question. Additionally, the logical outgrowth challenge is strong if the final rule contains significant provisions not reasonably foreseeable from the proposed rule.

Complete Process Case Study

The Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) proposes a rule requiring all manufacturers of children's toys to conduct third-party testing for 20 specific chemicals. During the comment period, many manufacturers submit studies questioning the scientific basis for testing requirements for 5 of these chemicals, arguing they're not harmful in the concentrations found in toys. The CPSC issues a final rule that maintains all 20 chemical testing requirements, stating only that it "disagrees with the industry studies." A trade association files a petition for review in the D.C. Circuit.

Question: Trace the complete administrative process and judicial review sequence for this rule, identifying key issues at each stage.

Administrative and Judicial Process Analysis:
  1. Rulemaking Initiation:
    • The CPSC initiates the rulemaking process, likely based on statutory authority in the Consumer Product Safety Act.
    • Key Issue: Does the statute specifically authorize the CPSC to regulate these 20 chemicals, or is this based on general product safety authority?
  2. Notice of Proposed Rulemaking:
    • The CPSC publishes the proposed rule in the Federal Register, explaining the basis and purpose.
    • Key Issue: Did the notice adequately explain the factual and scientific basis for regulating all 20 chemicals?
  3. Comment Period:
    • Manufacturers submit comments with scientific studies questioning the basis for 5 of the chemicals.
    • Key Issue: Were these comments "significant" under Portland Cement v. Ruckelshaus, requiring the agency to respond meaningfully?
  4. Final Rule Publication:
    • The CPSC issues a final rule maintaining all 20 chemical testing requirements.
    • The agency dismisses industry studies with a cursory statement of disagreement.
    • Key Issue: Did the agency provide a reasoned explanation for rejecting the industry studies?
  5. Petition for Review:
    • The trade association files in the D.C. Circuit, likely within the statutory 60-day window.
    • Key Issue: Does the association have standing to represent its members? (Likely yes, as the testing requirements directly affect manufacturers)
  6. Judicial Review — Procedural Challenges:
    • The court examines whether the CPSC followed proper procedural requirements, including adequate notice and opportunity to comment.
    • Key Issue: Did the CPSC respond adequately to significant comments as required by the APA?
  7. Judicial Review — Arbitrary & Capricious Analysis:
    • The court conducts "hard look" review under State Farm, examining whether the agency considered relevant factors and articulated a rational connection between facts found and choices made.
    • Key Issue: Is the CPSC's dismissal of scientific studies with a mere statement of disagreement sufficient under State Farm?
  8. Judicial Review — Statutory Authority:
    • The court examines whether the CPSC has statutory authority for its testing requirements.
    • Key Issue: Does the statute authorize mandatory third-party testing for these specific chemicals?
  9. Court Decision:
    • The court would likely find the final rule arbitrary and capricious with respect to the 5 contested chemicals.
    • Key Issue: Should the court vacate the entire rule or only the portions related to the 5 contested chemicals?
  10. Remedy & Remand:
    • The court would likely remand to the agency to provide a more reasoned explanation or reconsider the requirements for the 5 chemicals.
    • Key Issue: Will the court vacate the rule during remand or leave it in effect?
  11. Agency Response to Remand:
    • The CPSC might: (1) Better explain its rejection of the industry studies; (2) Modify the rule to exclude some chemicals; or (3) Conduct additional scientific review.
    • Key Issue: If the CPSC makes significant changes, would a new notice and comment period be required?

Likely Outcome: The court would likely find the CPSC's cursory dismissal of scientific studies arbitrary and capricious under State Farm, which requires agencies to examine relevant data and articulate a satisfactory explanation for their actions. The court would almost certainly remand for further explanation or reconsideration of the 5 contested chemicals. The court might sever and uphold the requirements for the 15 uncontested chemicals while vacating the requirements for the 5 contested chemicals.

Key Administrative Law Principles Illustrated: This scenario demonstrates the importance of reasoned agency decision-making, the requirement to respond meaningfully to significant comments, and how courts enforce these requirements through arbitrary and capricious review. It also illustrates the court's remedial flexibility in administrative law cases.

Expertise vs. Democratic Accountability

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issues a rule establishing stringent air quality standards based on complex scientific studies about the health effects of particulate matter. The rule is projected to save thousands of lives annually but will cost certain industries billions of dollars to implement. During the previous administration, the EPA had proposed but not finalized much less stringent standards based on different scientific models. The new administration explicitly cites policy priorities of environmental protection and climate change mitigation as factors in its decision. Industry groups challenge the rule, arguing the EPA inappropriately considered political factors rather than making a purely scientific determination.

Question: How should courts balance agency expertise with democratic accountability through the political branches? What legal standards apply?

Policy Tension Analysis:
  1. Tension Between Expertise and Politics:
    • Administrative agencies embody a tension: they are expert bodies with technical knowledge, but also part of the executive branch subject to presidential policy direction.
    • Pure technocracy would ignore democratic accountability; pure politics would undermine expertise-based decision-making.
    • The tension is particularly acute in science-based regulations with significant policy implications.
  2. Supreme Court Guidance on the Balance:
    • In State Farm, the Court suggested that purely political considerations cannot justify regulatory action without reasoned explanation.
    • However, in Fox Television, Justice Scalia noted that a change in administration and its policy priorities is a permissible basis for regulatory change, so long as the agency acknowledges the change and provides good reasons.
    • Massachusetts v. EPA clarified that while agencies have discretion, they must ground decisions in the statutory criteria, not extra-statutory policy considerations.
  3. Application to the EPA Air Quality Standards:
    • The Clean Air Act typically requires the EPA to set air quality standards based on public health considerations with an "adequate margin of safety," not economic costs.
    • If the EPA explicitly relied on climate change policy (not specifically mentioned in the relevant CAA provisions), this could be problematic.
    • However, if the agency based its decision on scientific evidence about health effects while acknowledging that the administration's priorities influenced its approach to uncertainty in the evidence, this would likely be permissible.
  4. Key Legal Standards:
    • The EPA must demonstrate that its standards are based on the statutory criteria (health effects) and supported by substantial evidence.
    • The agency must explain why it chose its approach over alternatives, including the previous administration's proposed standards.
    • If scientific evidence could support multiple conclusions, the agency may choose among reasonable alternatives, but must explain its reasoning.
    • If relying on new scientific studies, the agency must explain why these studies warrant a different outcome.
  5. Proper vs. Improper Political Considerations:
    • Proper: Acknowledging administration priorities influenced how the agency weighed uncertain scientific evidence or exercised discretion within statutory bounds.
    • Improper: Basing the decision on factors not contemplated by the statute or ignoring relevant scientific evidence for political reasons.
    • The distinction is often subtle but crucial for judicial review.

Resolution Framework: Courts should apply a balanced approach that acknowledges the legitimacy of political direction while ensuring agencies adhere to statutory mandates and reasoned decision-making:

  1. Examine whether the agency based its decision on factors that Congress intended it to consider in the authorizing statute.
  2. Review whether the agency examined the relevant scientific data and articulated a satisfactory explanation connecting that data to its conclusion.
  3. Acknowledge that when scientific evidence permits multiple reasonable interpretations, the administration's policy preferences may legitimately influence which reasonable interpretation the agency adopts.
  4. Require transparency about the role of policy considerations in the decision-making process.
  5. Ensure the agency properly explained any change in position from the previous administration.

Likely Outcome: If the EPA transparently explained its scientific basis while acknowledging policy considerations influenced its approach within statutory bounds, a court would likely uphold the rule. However, if the EPA explicitly relied on climate change considerations not contemplated by the Clean Air Act's national ambient air quality standards provisions, or if it failed to adequately explain its departure from the previous administration's approach, a court might find the decision arbitrary and capricious.

Advanced Hypotheticals

These challenging hypotheticals test your ability to apply multiple doctrines to novel scenarios that push the boundaries of existing precedent.

Novel Agency Design Challenge

Congress creates the Emerging Technologies Commission (ETC) with a unique structure and authority. The ETC has seven commissioners: three appointed by the President with Senate confirmation, two appointed directly by the Speaker of the House, and two appointed directly by the Senate Majority Leader. Commissioners serve 8-year terms and can only be removed for "gross misconduct" by a supermajority vote of the commission itself. The ETC has authority to regulate "emerging technologies that pose significant risks to national security or public welfare," with power to ban technologies, mandate safety features, or impose operational restrictions. Its funding comes from a dedicated tax on technology companies based on their market capitalization. The ETC issues a rule requiring all artificial intelligence systems above a certain capability threshold to include monitoring features allowing government oversight of training processes and internal operations. The rule also prohibits deployment of certain AI capabilities deemed "high risk." A technology company challenges both the ETC's structure and its AI rule.

Question: Analyze the constitutional and administrative law issues raised by this novel agency design and regulatory action.

Multi-Doctrinal Analysis:
  1. Appointments Clause Violations:
    • The appointment of commissioners directly by the Speaker and Senate Majority Leader clearly violates the Appointments Clause.
    • Under Buckley v. Valeo and subsequent cases, principal officers must be appointed by the President with Senate confirmation.
    • Even if commissioners were deemed inferior officers (unlikely given their authority and tenure), they would still need to be appointed by the President, department heads, or courts of law, not by legislators.
  2. Separation of Powers - Removal Restrictions:
    • The self-removal mechanism (commission supermajority vote) contravenes the President's Article II authority.
    • Under Free Enterprise Fund, multiple layers of removal protection are unconstitutional.
    • The "gross misconduct" standard imposes a higher removal threshold than the typical "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance" standard upheld for independent agencies.
  3. Legislative Branch Encroachment:
    • Direct legislative appointment of executive officers violates basic separation of powers principles.
    • This structure gives Congress control over executive functions, contrary to Bowsher v. Synar's prohibition on congressional control over execution of laws.
  4. Non-Appropriations Funding:
    • The dedicated tax funding mechanism raises Appropriations Clause concerns.
    • By circumventing annual appropriations, this structure diminishes congressional fiscal control.
Statutory Authority & Regulatory Action Analysis:
  1. Nondelegation Concerns:
    • The broad authority to regulate "emerging technologies that pose significant risks" may lack an intelligible principle.
    • The statute provides minimal guidance on what constitutes "significant risks."
    • While the Supreme Court has rarely invalidated statutes on nondelegation grounds, this broad delegation may be vulnerable under recent judicial skepticism.
  2. Major Questions Doctrine:
    • Regulation of AI capabilities and mandating government monitoring features presents a question of vast economic and political significance.
    • The ETC would need clear congressional authorization for such sweeping regulation.
    • The general authority over "emerging technologies" likely lacks the specificity required under West Virginia v. EPA.
  3. First Amendment Implications:
    • Government monitoring of AI systems may implicate First Amendment concerns, particularly if AI systems generate speech or expression.
    • Prohibition on certain AI capabilities could restrict development of tools for artistic, journalistic, or expressive purposes.
  4. Fourth Amendment Considerations:
    • Mandatory monitoring features allowing government oversight may constitute warrantless surveillance.
    • The rule would need to address Fourth Amendment protections against unreasonable searches.

Likely Outcome: The ETC's structure is clearly unconstitutional on multiple grounds, most obviously the Appointments Clause violation through legislative appointment of executive officers. A court would almost certainly invalidate the agency's structure. The AI regulation would likely fall as a result, though even if considered separately, it would face serious Major Questions Doctrine challenges, as the sweeping regulation of AI technology would require clear congressional authorization.

Severability Questions: A key question would be whether the unconstitutional structural provisions are severable from the rest of the statute. Given the fundamental nature of the Appointments Clause violation and its centrality to the agency's design, a court would likely find the entire agency structure inseverable and invalid. Even if the structural provisions were somehow severable, the AI rule would likely exceed the agency's statutory authority under the Major Questions Doctrine.

Approaching Administrative Law Exams

Administrative law exams typically present complex fact patterns that raise multiple issues across various doctrines. Success requires not only knowing the substantive law but also recognizing which issues to address and in what order. This section provides a structured approach for analyzing administrative law questions.

General Approach to Admin Law Questions

  1. Identify the Agency Action: What is the agency doing (or not doing)? Is it rulemaking, adjudication, informal guidance, or something else?
  2. Consider Threshold Issues: Can a court even hear this case?
    • Reviewability (Is the action reviewable or "committed to agency discretion"?)
    • Standing (Does the plaintiff have Article III standing and fall within the zone of interests?)
    • Ripeness (Is the case ready for review or premature?)
    • Finality (Is the agency action "final" under the APA?)
    • Exhaustion (Has the plaintiff exhausted administrative remedies?)
  3. Analyze Procedural Issues: Did the agency follow proper procedures?
    • If rulemaking: Were notice-and-comment requirements followed? Was the action exempt?
    • If adjudication: Was formal adjudication required? Were proper procedures followed?
  4. Assess Substantive Issues: Was the agency's action legally valid?
    • Constitutional issues (nondelegation, major questions, separation of powers)
    • Statutory interpretation (Skidmore analysis after Loper Bright; whether the agency's interpretation is the best reading of the statute)
    • Arbitrary and capricious review (State Farm factors)
  5. Analyze Potential Remedies: If the agency action is invalid, what should the court do?
    • Remand without vacatur vs. vacatur
    • Nationwide injunction vs. as-applied relief

  2. Be Methodical
    • Don't jump straight to the "interesting" issue while skipping prerequisites

  3. Use Cases Precisely
    • Cite relevant cases by name

  • Focusing exclusively on seminal cases while ignoring recent developments that modify their holdings
  • Applying pre-Loper Bright analysis to statutory interpretation questions
  • Using generic statements without tying analysis to specific facts of the problem

Doctrine Checklist
Constitutional Framework
  • Nondelegation Doctrine
    • Does statute provide an "intelligible principle"?
    • How detailed is the statutory guidance?
    • Does the delegation raise concerns about private delegation?
  • Major Questions Doctrine
    • Does agency claim authority over question of vast economic and political significance?
    • Is there clear congressional authorization?
    • Is this a "transformative expansion" of authority?
    • Has Congress considered and rejected similar proposals?
  • Presidential Control
    • Is the President's removal power restricted?
    • Is this a multimember commission or single director?
    • Are there multiple layers of removal protection?
  • Congressional Control
    • Does Congress try to control agency without bicameralism and presentment?
    • Is Congress using constitutional tools (appropriations, oversight, advice and consent)?
Agency Procedures
  • Rulemaking Choice
    • Does the agency have authority to issue rules?
    • Is the agency proceeding through rulemaking or adjudication?
    • Is the choice within the agency's discretion (Bell Aerospace)?
  • Notice and Comment Rulemaking
    • Did the agency provide adequate notice of the proposed rule?
    • Did the agency consider significant comments?
    • Is there a logical connection between the facts and the rule adopted?
    • Did the agency provide a reasoned explanation?
  • Exceptions to Notice and Comment
    • Is the rule interpretive rather than legislative?
    • Is it a general statement of policy that genuinely preserves discretion?
    • Is it a procedural rule that doesn't substantially affect rights?
    • Is there good cause to bypass notice and comment?
  • Formal Adjudication
    • Does the statute require a decision "on the record after opportunity for an agency hearing"?
    • Were APA §§ 554, 556, and 557 procedures followed?
Judicial Review
  • Statutory Interpretation (post-Loper Bright)
    • What is the best reading of the statute using traditional tools of interpretation?
    • Has the agency provided expertise or considered views deserving of respect?
    • Is the interpretation consistent, longstanding, and well-reasoned?
  • Arbitrary and Capricious Review
    • Did the agency examine relevant data and articulate a satisfactory explanation?
    • Did the agency consider important aspects of the problem?
    • Is the explanation consistent with the evidence?
    • Did the agency consider obvious alternatives?
    • If changing course, did the agency acknowledge the change and provide good reasons?
  • Substantial Evidence Review
    • Is there "such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept"?
    • Does the record as a whole support the agency's factual findings?
  • Regulatory Interpretation (Kisor Deference)
    • Is the regulation genuinely ambiguous after exhausting traditional interpretation tools?
    • Is the agency's interpretation reasonable?
    • Is it the agency's authoritative position?
    • Does it implicate agency expertise?
    • Does it reflect fair and considered judgment?
Access to Judicial Review
  • Reviewability
    • Does a statute preclude review?
    • Is the action committed to agency discretion by law?
    • Is this an enforcement decision presumptively unreviewable under Heckler?
  • Standing
    • Is there injury in fact (concrete, particularized, actual or imminent)?
    • Is the injury fairly traceable to the challenged action?
    • Is it likely to be redressed by a favorable decision?
    • Does plaintiff fall within the "zone of interests" protected by the statute?
    • Is the plaintiff a state with "special solicitude"?
  • Ripeness
    • Is the issue fit for judicial decision (purely legal, final action)?
    • Would withholding review cause hardship to the parties?
  • Finality
    • Does the action mark the consummation of the agency's decision-making process?
    • Is it one by which rights or obligations have been determined or from which legal consequences flow?
  • Exhaustion
    • Has the plaintiff exhausted administrative remedies?
    • Does a statute or regulation mandate exhaustion?
    • Do exceptions to exhaustion apply (futility, inadequate remedies, bias)?

Hypothetical 1: Major Questions Doctrine

Facts: The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issues a rule requiring all social media platforms with more than 10 million users to implement content moderation policies that remove false or misleading information within 24 hours. The FCC relies on a provision in its organic statute authorizing it to "make such rules and regulations... as may be necessary in the execution of its functions." Several tech companies challenge the rule.

Questions:

  1. Would the Major Questions Doctrine apply to this agency action? Why or why not?
  2. How would the factors from West Virginia v. EPA apply?
  3. Would your analysis change if Congress had specifically authorized the FCC to "regulate social media platforms to prevent the spread of misinformation"?
Hypothetical 2: Arbitrary and Capricious Review

Facts: The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issues a rule requiring warning labels on high-sugar beverages. The rule applies to sodas but exempts flavored coffee drinks and fruit juices, even though they often contain comparable amounts of sugar. In the rule's statement of basis and purpose, the FDA states that "sugary beverages pose health risks" but doesn't address why coffee drinks and fruit juices are exempt. A soda manufacturer challenges the rule as arbitrary and capricious.

Questions:

  1. What arguments would the soda manufacturer make under State Farm?
  2. Would the FDA's rule likely be upheld or invalidated? Why?
  3. What could the FDA have done differently to strengthen its rule against challenge?
Hypothetical 3: Standing

Facts: The Department of the Interior (DOI) lifts a ban on oil and gas leasing in a national wildlife refuge in Alaska. An environmental group whose members visit the refuge occasionally for bird-watching challenges the decision. The group argues that oil and gas development will harm wildlife and reduce their members' enjoyment of the area. DOI argues that the plaintiffs lack standing because development hasn't started yet and may never affect the specific areas the members visit.

Questions:

  1. Do the environmental group's members have standing under Lujan? Why or why not?
  2. How might the plaintiffs strengthen their standing arguments?
  3. Would the analysis change if the plaintiff was the State of Alaska rather than an environmental group?
Hypothetical 4: Post-Loper Bright Statutory Interpretation

Facts: The Clean Water Act prohibits discharge of pollutants into "navigable waters," defined as "waters of the United States." The EPA and Army Corps of Engineers issue a rule interpreting this phrase to include wetlands with a "significant nexus" to traditionally navigable waters. A developer who wants to build on wetlands challenges the rule, arguing that "waters of the United States" should be limited to permanent, standing, or flowing bodies of water.

Questions:

  1. How would a court analyze this challenge after Loper Bright?
  2. What traditional tools of statutory interpretation would be relevant?
  3. What role would the agencies' expertise play in the court's analysis?
