Chevron Doctrine

What was the two-step framework for judicial review of agency statutory interpretations?

Chevron Doctrine

Step 1: Has Congress directly spoken to the precise question at issue? If yes, that's the end of the matter.

Step 2: If the statute is silent or ambiguous, is the agency's interpretation reasonable?

Note: Loper Bright (2024) overturned this framework.

Major Questions Doctrine

When does the Major Questions Doctrine apply, and what does it require?

Major Questions Doctrine

Applies when an agency claims authority over a question of vast economic and political significance.

Requires: Clear congressional authorization for the agency's action.

Key cases: West Virginia v. EPA, NFIB v. OSHA

Article III Standing

What are the three requirements for Article III standing?

Article III Standing

1. Injury in fact: Concrete, particularized, actual or imminent

2. Causation: Fairly traceable to the challenged action

3. Redressability: Likely to be redressed by a favorable decision

Zone of interests is a separate prudential requirement.

Self-Assessment Quizzes

Test your knowledge with these multiple-choice questions covering key administrative law concepts.

1. Which of the following is NOT a requirement for Article III standing?

Correct Answer: B. Final agency action

Final agency action is a requirement for reviewability under the APA, not for Article III standing. The three requirements for Article III standing are injury in fact, causation, and redressability.

2. After Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024), how should courts approach agency statutory interpretations?

Correct Answer: B. Determine the best reading of the statute using traditional tools of interpretation

In Loper Bright, the Supreme Court overturned Chevron deference. Courts now must determine the best reading of a statute using traditional tools of statutory interpretation, rather than deferring to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes.

3. When is an agency's decision "committed to agency discretion by law" and thus unreviewable?

Correct Answer: B. When there is "no law to apply" to guide judicial review

Under APA § 701(a)(2), agency action is "committed to agency discretion by law" when the statute provides no meaningful standard against which to judge the agency's exercise of discretion. This is known as the "no law to apply" test from Citizens to Preserve Overton Park v. Volpe.

Practice Arena

Apply your knowledge and test your understanding with these practice scenarios. Work through these examples to strengthen your ability to analyze administrative law problems under exam conditions.

Scenario 1: Agency Authority

The Department of Transportation (DOT) issues a rule requiring all smartphone manufacturers to include a feature that automatically disables text messaging capabilities when a phone is detected moving faster than 10 mph. The DOT claims authority under a statute that authorizes it to "issue regulations to enhance highway safety." Several technology companies challenge the rule.

Question: What doctrine(s) would you analyze to determine if DOT has the authority to issue this rule?

Analysis:

  1. Major Questions Doctrine: This is likely a question of vast economic and political significance, as it would impact every smartphone manufacturer and user in the country. Under this doctrine, the court would look for clear congressional authorization for DOT to regulate smartphone functionality.
  2. Statutory Authority: Does the broad language "enhance highway safety" clearly authorize regulation of smartphone manufacturing, which is not traditionally within DOT's regulatory sphere?
  3. Nondelegation Concerns: Is "enhance highway safety" an intelligible principle sufficient to guide the agency's discretion for this type of regulation?

Given recent Supreme Court cases like West Virginia v. EPA, a court would likely find that Congress did not clearly authorize DOT to impose design requirements on smartphones, even if the purpose relates to highway safety.

Scenario 2: Arbitrary and Capricious Review

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issues a final rule reducing the permissible levels of a certain air pollutant from industrial sources. In its rulemaking, the EPA discusses the health benefits of the regulation but doesn't address industry concerns about technological feasibility or economic impacts raised during the comment period. An industry group challenges the rule as arbitrary and capricious.

Question: How would a court likely analyze this challenge under State Farm?

Analysis:

Under Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm, a court would likely find this rule arbitrary and capricious because:

  1. The EPA failed to consider an important aspect of the problem (technological feasibility and economic impacts)
  2. The agency did not respond to significant comments raised during the notice-and-comment period
  3. The agency's explanation is incomplete, as it addresses only the benefits without considering the costs or implementation challenges

The court would likely remand to the agency to address these deficiencies in the rulemaking process, requiring the EPA to consider and respond to the industry concerns before finalizing the rule.

Hypothetical 1: Major Questions Doctrine

Facts: The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issues a rule requiring all social media platforms with more than 10 million users to implement content moderation policies that remove false or misleading information within 24 hours. The FCC relies on a provision in its organic statute authorizing it to "make such rules and regulations... as may be necessary in the execution of its functions." Several tech companies challenge the rule.

Questions:

  1. Would the Major Questions Doctrine apply to this agency action? Why or why not?
  2. How would the factors from West Virginia v. EPA apply?
  3. Would your analysis change if Congress had specifically authorized the FCC to "regulate social media platforms to prevent the spread of misinformation"?

Hypothetical 2: Standing

Facts: The Department of the Interior (DOI) lifts a ban on oil and gas leasing in a national wildlife refuge in Alaska. An environmental group whose members visit the refuge occasionally for bird-watching challenges the decision. The group argues that oil and gas development will harm wildlife and reduce their members' enjoyment of the area. DOI argues that the plaintiffs lack standing because development hasn't started yet and may never affect the specific areas the members visit.

Questions:

  1. Do the environmental group's members have standing under Lujan? Why or why not?
  2. How might the plaintiffs strengthen their standing arguments?
  3. Would the analysis change if the plaintiff was the State of Alaska rather than an environmental group?

Exam Strategies & Analysis Frameworks

Develop a systematic approach to administrative law exam questions with these proven strategies, issue-spotting guides, and doctrine checklists.

Approaching Administrative Law Exams

Administrative law exams typically present complex fact patterns that raise multiple issues across various doctrines. Success requires not only knowing the substantive law but also recognizing which issues to address and in what order. This section provides a structured approach for analyzing administrative law questions.

General Approach to Admin Law Questions

  1. Identify the Agency Action: What is the agency doing (or not doing)? Is it rulemaking, adjudication, informal guidance, or something else?
  2. Consider Threshold Issues: Can a court even hear this case?
    • Reviewability (Is the action reviewable or "committed to agency discretion"?)
    • Standing (Does the plaintiff have Article III standing and fall within the zone of interests?)
    • Ripeness (Is the case ready for review or premature?)
    • Finality (Is the agency action "final" under the APA?)
    • Exhaustion (Has the plaintiff exhausted administrative remedies?)
  3. Analyze Procedural Issues: Did the agency follow proper procedures?
    • If rulemaking: Were notice-and-comment requirements followed? Was the action exempt?
    • If adjudication: Was formal adjudication required? Were proper procedures followed?
  4. Assess Substantive Issues: Was the agency's action legally valid?
    • Constitutional issues (nondelegation, major questions, separation of powers)
    • Statutory interpretation (Skidmore analysis after Loper Bright; whether the agency's interpretation is the best reading of the statute)
    • Arbitrary and capricious review (State Farm factors)
  5. Analyze Potential Remedies: If the agency action is invalid, what should the court do?
    • Remand without vacatur vs. vacatur
    • Nationwide injunction vs. as-applied relief

Exam Strategy and Tips

General Exam Strategies

  1. Organize Your Answer
    • Use headings and subheadings to structure your response
    • Consider using the checklist approach detailed above
    • Start with threshold issues before addressing the merits
  2. Be Methodical
    • Don't jump straight to the "interesting" issue while skipping prerequisites
    • Apply tests step by step, rather than jumping to conclusions
    • Consider both sides of close issues
  3. Use Cases Precisely
    • Cite relevant cases by name
    • Explain why a case is analogous or distinguishable
    • Focus on reasoning, not just holdings
  4. Mind Recent Developments
    • Pay special attention to recent Supreme Court decisions that modify doctrines
    • Note when a longstanding precedent has been overturned (e.g., Loper Bright)
  5. Manage Your Time
    • Allocate time based on the points/percentage assigned to each question
    • Don't get stuck on a single issue for too long
    • If time is running short, outline your remaining analysis

Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing different standards of review (substantial evidence vs. arbitrary and capricious)
  • Failing to address threshold issues before reaching the merits
  • Jumping to conclusions without applying the full test or framework
  • Stating only the majority position without acknowledging split decisions or evolving doctrines
  • Focusing exclusively on seminal cases while ignoring recent developments that modify their holdings
  • Applying pre-Loper Bright analysis to statutory interpretation questions
  • Using generic statements without tying analysis to specific facts of the problem
