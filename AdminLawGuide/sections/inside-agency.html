Rulemaking Authority and Agency Choice

Administrative agencies derive their authority to make rules from their organic statutes—the laws that create the agencies and define their powers. An agency cannot create rules unless Congress has authorized it to do so, either explicitly or implicitly.

National Petroleum Refiners Association v. FTC (D.C. Cir. 1973)

482 F.2d 672

The FTC promulgated a rule declaring that failure to post octane ratings on gas pumps was an unfair practice. The National Petroleum Refiners Association challenged the FTC's authority to issue substantive rules rather than proceeding through case-by-case adjudication.

The D.C. Circuit upheld the FTC's substantive rulemaking power, finding that it had implicit authority under its organic statute to promulgate substantive rules, not just procedural ones.

The court reasoned that:

  • Agencies have good reasons to prefer rulemaking over adjudication in many instances
  • Rulemaking is more efficient, allowing the agency to address issues comprehensively rather than piecemeal
  • Rulemaking provides greater notice to regulated parties
  • Rulemaking allows for broader public participation
  • The statutory language and legislative history did not preclude substantive rulemaking

The APA's Definition of a "Rule"

The Administrative Procedure Act (APA) defines a "rule" in 5 U.S.C. § 551(4) as:

"[T]he whole or a part of an agency statement of general or particular applicability and future effect designed to implement, interpret, or prescribe law or policy or describing the organization, procedure, or practice requirements of an agency..."

This broad definition encompasses various types of agency pronouncements, including:

  • Legislative Rules: Binding regulations with the force of law
  • Interpretive Rules: Agency interpretations of statutes or regulations
  • Policy Statements: General statements of agency policy
  • Procedural Rules: Rules governing agency procedures

Rulemaking vs. Adjudication

Agencies can establish policy through either rulemaking or adjudication. The choice between these procedures involves important tradeoffs and may significantly affect both the agency's ability to accomplish its goals and the rights of regulated parties.

Rulemaking Adjudication
Generally prospective Primarily retrospective
Applies to all regulated parties Applies to specific parties
Broad public participation Limited to parties and intervenors
More efficient for setting general policy More efficient for resolving individual disputes
Better for technical, scientific issues Better for fact-intensive, case-specific issues
Clearer advance notice to regulated parties Potential retroactive application

Londoner v. Denver (1908)

210 U.S. 373

Denver assessed property owners for the cost of paving the street adjacent to their properties. The city did not provide property owners with notice or an opportunity for an individualized hearing before imposing assessments.

The Supreme Court held that due process required notice and an opportunity to be heard before the assessment became final because the assessment process involved individualized determinations affecting specific property owners.

Bi-Metallic Investment Co. v. State Board of Equalization (1915)

239 U.S. 441

Colorado's State Board of Equalization ordered a 40% increase in the valuation of all taxable property in Denver. Property owners challenged this order, arguing they were entitled to individualized hearings.

The Supreme Court held that due process did not require individualized hearings for general rules affecting many people. Justice Holmes distinguished Londoner, explaining that when government decisions affect large numbers of people, the political process, not individualized hearings, is the appropriate remedy.

Londoner and Bi-Metallic: The Distinction Between Adjudicative and Legislative Facts

These cases establish a fundamental distinction in administrative law:

  • Adjudicative Facts: Facts about specific parties (who did what, where, when, how, why) that warrant procedural protections
  • Legislative Facts: General facts that help determine questions of law, policy, and discretion (not requiring individualized hearings)

When an agency action involves adjudicative facts concerning specific individuals, due process may require hearing rights even if the APA does not. When an action involves legislative facts affecting many people in the same way, due process typically does not require individualized hearings.

NLRB v. Bell Aerospace Co. (1974)

416 U.S. 267

The National Labor Relations Board reversed its long-standing position that buyers were "managerial employees" excluded from the National Labor Relations Act's protection. Bell Aerospace argued that this significant policy change required rulemaking rather than adjudication.

The Supreme Court held that the NLRB had discretion to establish new policy through adjudication rather than rulemaking, even when overturning longstanding precedent. The choice between rulemaking and adjudication "lies primarily in the informed discretion of the administrative agency."

The Court noted that:

  • The NLRB has traditionally developed its policies through adjudication
  • The determination of whether buyers are "managerial employees" involves facts that may vary from case to case
  • No significant reliance interests would be harmed by the retroactive application of the new policy
  • The new policy did not involve fines or damages

NLRB v. Wyman-Gordon Co. (1969)

394 U.S. 759

In an adjudication involving another company (Excelsior Underwear), the NLRB announced a new rule requiring employers to provide unions with employee names and addresses before representation elections. The NLRB then ordered Wyman-Gordon to comply with this rule in a subsequent proceeding. Wyman-Gordon challenged this order, arguing that the rule was invalid because it was not promulgated through proper rulemaking procedures.

The Supreme Court upheld the NLRB's order requiring Wyman-Gordon to provide the employee list, but a plurality criticized the Board's practice of announcing general rules in adjudications without following APA rulemaking procedures.

Justice Fortas's plurality opinion:

  • Criticized the NLRB for attempting to create a rule of general applicability through adjudication rather than following APA rulemaking procedures
  • Acknowledged that agencies may choose between rulemaking and adjudication, but emphasized that they cannot use adjudication to circumvent the APA's rulemaking requirements
  • Distinguished between announcing a new principle in an adjudication that applies to the parties in that case, and creating a rule announced for future cases only
  • Upheld the order against Wyman-Gordon because it was directed specifically at that company, not merely an application of the Excelsior rule

Notice and Comment Rulemaking

The Administrative Procedure Act establishes procedures for agency rulemaking, with "informal" or "notice-and-comment" rulemaking under § 553 being the most common form. This process balances the need for agency flexibility with the values of public participation and accountability.

APA § 553: Notice and Comment Rulemaking

The key requirements include:

  1. Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM): Published in the Federal Register, including:
    • Statement of the time, place, and nature of the proceedings
    • Reference to the legal authority under which the rule is proposed
    • Either the terms or substance of the proposed rule or a description of the subjects and issues involved
  2. Comment Period: The agency must give interested persons an opportunity to participate through submission of written data, views, or arguments
  3. Consideration of Comments: The agency must consider the relevant material submitted
  4. Statement of Basis and Purpose: The agency must incorporate in the final rule a "concise general statement of their basis and purpose"
  5. Publication and Effective Date: The final rule must be published at least 30 days before its effective date (with certain exceptions)

United States v. Nova Scotia Food Products Corp. (2d Cir. 1977)

568 F.2d 240

The FDA used notice-and-comment rulemaking to promulgate regulations aimed at preventing botulism in smoked fish. Nova Scotia Food Products refused to comply with the regulations, arguing that they would destroy its fish without significantly reducing the risk of botulism.

The Second Circuit held that the FDA's rulemaking was procedurally defective because the agency failed to disclose the scientific data upon which it relied and failed to respond to significant comments. The court invalidated the rule.

The court concluded that:

  • An agency must disclose the data and studies on which it relies during rulemaking
  • An agency must respond to significant comments that are relevant to the rulemaking
  • The "concise general statement of basis and purpose" must be sufficiently detailed to allow for meaningful judicial review
  • Failure to follow these procedures undermines the fairness and rationality of the rulemaking process

Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Corp. v. NRDC (1978)

435 U.S. 519

The Atomic Energy Commission (AEC) conducted rulemaking on the environmental effects of the nuclear fuel cycle. The D.C. Circuit held that the procedures used by the AEC were inadequate, even though they satisfied the APA's requirements, and suggested additional procedures the agency should have used.

The Supreme Court unanimously reversed, holding that courts cannot impose procedural requirements beyond those in the APA. The APA establishes the maximum procedural requirements that courts may impose on agencies.

Justice Rehnquist's opinion emphasized that:

  • Agencies have discretion to decide what procedures to use beyond the APA's minimum requirements
  • Courts lack authority to require additional procedures based on their view of what would be "best"
  • Judicial imposition of additional procedures would make administrative proceedings "totally unpredictable"
  • Policy considerations favor allowing agencies to determine appropriate procedures

The Court described the D.C. Circuit's approach as "judicial intervention run riot."

After Vermont Yankee, courts cannot impose procedures beyond those in the APA. However, they can ensure that agencies comply with the APA's requirements, as interpreted in cases like Nova Scotia. The result is a form of "hard look" review that focuses on the adequacy of the agency's reasoning rather than the procedures it followed.

Exceptions to Notice and Comment

The APA exempts certain types of rules from the notice-and-comment requirements of § 553. These exceptions provide agencies with flexibility when formal procedures would be impractical, unnecessary, or contrary to the public interest.

APA § 553(b) Exceptions

Notice and comment requirements do not apply to:

  1. Interpretive Rules: Agency statements that interpret existing law without changing legal obligations
  2. General Statements of Policy: Agency statements that indicate how the agency intends to exercise discretion in the future
  3. Rules of Agency Organization, Procedure, or Practice: Internal rules governing agency operations
  4. Good Cause Exception: When notice and comment is "impracticable, unnecessary, or contrary to the public interest"

American Mining Congress v. Mine Safety & Health Administration (D.C. Cir. 1993)

995 F.2d 1106

The Mine Safety and Health Administration issued a "Program Policy Letter" defining what constituted a "diagnosis" of black lung disease that had to be reported under existing regulations. The agency did not use notice-and-comment procedures, claiming the letter was an interpretive rule.

The D.C. Circuit held that the Program Policy Letter was indeed an interpretive rule exempt from notice-and-comment requirements. The court articulated a test for distinguishing interpretive rules from legislative rules.

Judge Williams proposed a test with three key questions for identifying a legislative rule:

  1. Would there be an adequate basis for enforcement action without the rule?
  2. Did the agency explicitly invoke its legislative authority?
  3. Does the rule effectively amend a prior legislative rule?

If the answer to any of these questions is "no," then the rule is legislative and requires notice and comment.

United States v. Texas (2023)

599 U.S. 577

The Department of Homeland Security announced a policy (the "DAPA program") that would allow undocumented immigrants who had been in the country for five years and had citizen or lawful permanent resident children to apply for deferred action. The agency did not use notice-and-comment procedures, claiming the policy was a general statement of policy.

The Supreme Court held that the DAPA program was not a general statement of policy exempt from notice-and-comment requirements because it appeared to bind agency officials rather than preserve their discretion.

The Court concluded that:

  • The program created binding norms that effectively determined how officials would exercise discretion
  • Nearly every application was approved, suggesting officials lacked meaningful discretion
  • The policy's formality and scope indicated it was not merely explaining how existing discretion would be exercised

Community Nutrition Institute v. Young (D.C. Cir. 1986)

818 F.2d 943

The FDA published "action levels" for unavoidable contaminants in food, stating that food containing contaminants below these levels would not be subject to enforcement action. The FDA claimed these action levels were policy statements exempt from notice-and-comment requirements.

The D.C. Circuit held that the action levels were actually legislative rules, not policy statements, because they had binding effect on the agency's enforcement discretion and created a "safe harbor" for regulated entities.

The court reasoned that:

  • Despite the FDA's characterization of action levels as non-binding, in practice they were treated as having binding effect
  • The FDA's regulations stated that food with contaminants exceeding action levels "will be deemed" to be adulterated, indicating they were not merely tentative
  • The action levels effectively bound the agency's enforcement discretion
  • The language and context of the action levels demonstrated they were intended to have legal consequences

Air Transport Association of America v. Department of Transportation (D.C. Cir. 1990)

900 F.2d 369

The FAA issued a "Statement of Enforcement Policy" addressing how it would implement its authority to assess civil penalties. The agency claimed the statement was an interpretive rule and policy statement exempt from notice-and-comment requirements.

The D.C. Circuit held that parts of the FAA's enforcement policy were interpretive rules properly exempt from notice-and-comment, while other parts were legislative rules requiring notice-and-comment procedures.

The court distinguished between:

  • Portions that merely explained the FAA's understanding of statutory terms (interpretive)
  • Portions that created new reporting requirements and obligations not found in the statute (legislative)
  • The court focused on whether the rule had legal effects beyond what the statute itself prescribed
  • The court emphasized that "the more expansive the regulatory reach of these rules, the greater the necessity for public comment"

Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Association (2015)

575 U.S. 92

The Department of Labor (DOL) issued an interpretation in 2010 that mortgage loan officers did not qualify for the administrative exemption under the Fair Labor Standards Act. This position reversed the DOL's 2006 opinion. The Mortgage Bankers Association challenged this reversal, arguing that the DOL had to use notice-and-comment procedures to change its interpretation.

The Supreme Court unanimously held that when an agency changes its interpretation of its own regulations, it is not required to use notice-and-comment rulemaking procedures. The APA expressly exempts interpretive rules from these requirements.

Justice Sotomayor's opinion:

  • The APA's plain text exempts interpretive rules from notice-and-comment requirements, regardless of whether they modify previous interpretations
  • The D.C. Circuit's "Paralyzed Veterans doctrine" (requiring notice and comment for changes in interpretation) had no basis in the APA's text
  • Interpretive rules, while binding on the agency itself, do not have the force of law
  • The APA contains other safeguards against arbitrary agency action, including arbitrary and capricious review

Distinguishing Between Types of Rules

Legislative Rules vs. Interpretive Rules

  • Legislative Rules: Create new law, rights, or duties; require notice and comment
  • Interpretive Rules: Explain or clarify existing law; exempt from notice and comment
  • Key Test: Could the agency enforce the underlying statute without the rule? If not, the rule is legislative.

Legislative Rules vs. General Statements of Policy

  • Legislative Rules: Establish binding norms; require notice and comment
  • General Statements of Policy: Announce tentative intentions for the future; exempt from notice and comment
  • Key Test: Does the rule leave agency officials genuine discretion, or does it effectively bind them? If it binds them, it's legislative.

Legislative Rules vs. Procedural Rules

  • Legislative Rules: Affect substantive rights; require notice and comment
  • Procedural Rules: Govern agency's internal operations; exempt from notice and comment
  • Key Test: Does the rule significantly affect parties' substantive rights or interests, or does it merely address how the agency will conduct its business?

Adjudication Under the APA

Adjudication is the process by which an agency makes a determination concerning the rights of specific parties in a particular matter. The APA establishes procedures for both formal and informal adjudication, with formal adjudication procedures outlined in §§ 554, 556, and 557.

APA § 554(a): When Formal Adjudication Applies

"This section applies... in every case of adjudication required by statute to be determined on the record after opportunity for an agency hearing..."

Dominion Energy Brayton Point, LLC v. Johnson (1st Cir. 2006)

443 F.3d 12

The Clean Water Act required the EPA to provide an "opportunity for public hearing" before issuing a variance permit. The EPA interpreted this provision as not requiring formal adjudication under the APA. Dominion Energy challenged this interpretation, citing a previous First Circuit case that had required formal procedures.

The First Circuit held that the EPA's interpretation was reasonable under Chevron deference, and that formal adjudication was not required. The court overruled its previous precedent based on the Supreme Court's intervening decision in Brand X, which held that a prior judicial interpretation does not override an agency's reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statute.

The court concluded that:

  • The phrase "opportunity for public hearing" does not necessarily mean "on the record" hearing
  • Under Chevron, the EPA's interpretation deserved deference
  • Under Brand X, the agency's reasonable interpretation could overrule prior judicial precedent that was not based on a finding that the statute was unambiguous

Key Features of Formal Adjudication

Pre-Hearing:

  • Notice to parties of time, place, nature of hearing, legal authority, and matters of fact and law asserted
  • Opportunity to submit facts, arguments, offers of settlement
  • Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) typically presides

Hearing:

  • Right to present evidence and conduct cross-examination
  • ALJ has authority to administer oaths, issue subpoenas, rule on evidence, regulate course of hearing
  • Burden of proof on proponent of order
  • Federal Rules of Evidence do not apply; all relevant evidence admissible

Post-Hearing:

  • ALJ issues either an "initial decision" (final unless appealed) or a "recommended decision" (agency must issue final decision)
  • Agency's review of ALJ decisions is effectively de novo
  • Final decision must include a statement of findings, conclusions, and reasons
  • Judicial review generally available under APA § 706

Union Electric Co. v. FERC (D.C. Cir. 1989)

890 F.2d 1193

In setting Union Electric's rate of return, FERC took official notice of a decline in interest rates that occurred after the ALJ's decision. Union Electric was not given an opportunity to challenge this factual determination before FERC issued its final decision.

The D.C. Circuit set aside FERC's decision because the agency violated APA § 556(e), which requires that parties be given an opportunity to show the contrary when an agency takes official notice of a material fact.

The court emphasized that:

  • The right to rebut officially noticed facts is a fundamental procedural protection
  • The agency must consider and address the party's rebuttal arguments
  • This procedural protection is essential for ensuring the accuracy of agency decisions

Due Process in Administrative Context

Administrative agencies must provide procedural due process when their actions impact protected liberty or property interests. The Supreme Court has developed a framework for determining what process is due in different administrative contexts.

Goldberg v. Kelly (1970)

397 U.S. 254

New York welfare recipients challenged the termination of their benefits without a pre-termination hearing. The state's procedures allowed benefits to be terminated after an informal meeting with a caseworker, with a full hearing available only after termination.

The Supreme Court held that due process requires a pre-termination hearing before welfare benefits can be terminated. The hearing must include notice, an opportunity to present evidence, the right to confront adverse witnesses, the right to counsel, and a decision based on the evidence.

Justice Brennan's opinion:

  • Welfare benefits are a statutory entitlement for eligible recipients and are more like property than a "gratuity"
  • The recipient's interest in continued benefits outweighs the government's interest in summary adjudication, given the crucial importance of welfare to survival
  • The risk of erroneous deprivation is high without a pre-termination hearing
  • The financial cost to the government does not outweigh the individual's need for procedural protections

Board of Regents v. Roth (1972)

408 U.S. 564

David Roth was hired as an assistant professor at a state university for a fixed term of one year. When the university decided not to rehire him for the next academic year, it provided no statement of reasons and no opportunity for a hearing. Roth claimed that this violated his due process rights.

The Supreme Court held that Roth had no due process right to a statement of reasons or a hearing because he had no protected property interest in continued employment beyond his one-year contract, and no liberty interest was implicated by the non-renewal.

Justice Stewart's opinion:

  • Established that due process protections apply only when there is a deprivation of "liberty" or "property" interests protected by the Fourteenth Amendment
  • Property interests are not created by the Constitution but by "existing rules or understandings that stem from an independent source such as state law"
  • Roth had no property interest because his appointment was for one year only, with no provision for renewal
  • Liberty interests might be implicated if the non-renewal had damaged Roth's reputation or foreclosed other employment opportunities, but no such allegations were made

Perry v. Sindermann (1972)

408 U.S. 593

Robert Sindermann was a teacher in the state college system for ten years under a series of one-year contracts. After his last contract was not renewed, he claimed he had a property interest in continued employment based on the college's policies and practices, which allegedly created a de facto tenure system.

The Supreme Court held that a property interest might exist even without formal tenure if there was a "legitimate claim of entitlement" based on rules or mutually explicit understandings. Sindermann was entitled to a hearing to prove the existence of such an understanding.

Justice Stewart's opinion:

  • Property interests can arise from implied contracts or informal practices that create a legitimate expectation of continued employment
  • The absence of formal tenure is not dispositive if the policies and practices of the institution create a de facto tenure system
  • Citing the college's faculty guide, which stated that faculty members should feel secure in their positions as long as they performed satisfactorily, as evidence of a possible property interest
  • Emphasized that proving the existence of such rules or understandings would not automatically entitle Sindermann to reinstatement but only to a hearing on the legitimacy of his claim

Mathews v. Eldridge (1976)

424 U.S. 319

George Eldridge's Social Security disability benefits were terminated after a review process that included written submissions but no evidentiary hearing. Eldridge argued that due process required a pre-termination hearing similar to that required for welfare benefits in Goldberg v. Kelly.

The Supreme Court held that due process does not require a pre-termination evidentiary hearing before disability benefits are terminated. The Court established a three-factor balancing test for determining what process is due in administrative proceedings.

Justice Powell's opinion articulated a three-factor balancing test:

  1. The private interest affected by the official action
  2. The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used and the probable value of additional procedural safeguards
  3. The government's interest, including the function involved and the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional procedural requirements

Applying these factors, the Court distinguished disability benefits from welfare:

  • Disability benefits are not based on financial need, so the hardship of termination is generally less severe than welfare
  • Disability determinations are based on medical reports and records, which are more objective and reliable than the witness credibility issues in welfare cases
  • The existing procedures (including notice, access to information, and opportunity for written submission) adequately protected against error
  • The administrative burden and cost of pre-termination hearings would be substantial

Due Process Analysis Framework

  1. Protected Interest Inquiry: Is there a protected liberty or property interest?
    • Property interests stem from "legitimate claims of entitlement" created by laws, rules, or understandings
    • Liberty interests include freedom from restraint and the right to engage in common occupations
  2. What Process Is Due: If a protected interest exists, apply the Mathews balancing test:
    • Private interest affected
    • Risk of erroneous deprivation and value of additional safeguards
    • Government's interest, including administrative burden

The fundamental requirements of due process are notice and an opportunity to be heard, but the specific procedures required depend on the Mathews factors. More formal procedures (like evidentiary hearings) are required when the private interest is substantial, the risk of error is high, and the administrative burden is manageable.
