Part IV: Access to Judicial Review

Reviewability Standing Ripeness Finality Exhaustion

Reviewability: When Courts Can Review Agency Actions

Before a court can apply substantive standards of review to an agency action, it must first determine whether the action is reviewable at all. The APA creates a presumption of judicial review, but several exceptions limit this presumption.

APA § 701: Application; Definitions

(a) This chapter applies, according to the provisions thereof, except to the extent that—

  1. statutes preclude judicial review; or
  2. agency action is committed to agency discretion by law.

Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner (1967)

387 U.S. 136

The FDA issued regulations requiring that prescription drug labels and advertisements include the generic name of the drug each time the brand name appeared. Pharmaceutical companies challenged these regulations before they were enforced against any specific company.

The Supreme Court established a presumption in favor of judicial review, holding that "judicial review of a final agency action by an aggrieved person will not be cut off unless there is persuasive reason to believe that such was the purpose of Congress."

Justice Harlan's opinion emphasized that:

  • The APA's "generous review provisions" should be given a "hospitable interpretation"
  • Only upon a showing of "clear and convincing evidence" of contrary legislative intent should courts restrict access to judicial review
  • The courts are the primary interpreters of federal statutes and must independently determine if Congress intended to preclude review

Heckler v. Chaney (1985)

470 U.S. 821

Death row inmates requested that the FDA take enforcement action against states using lethal injection drugs for allegedly violating the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act. The FDA declined to investigate, stating that it lacked jurisdiction and that it had discretion not to pursue enforcement actions.

The Supreme Court held that agency decisions not to take enforcement actions are presumptively unreviewable under APA § 701(a)(2) as actions "committed to agency discretion by law," because they involve balancing factors that are peculiarly within the agency's expertise.

Justice Rehnquist's opinion reasoned that:

  • Agency enforcement decisions are analogous to prosecutorial discretion, which has traditionally been unreviewable
  • Agencies must balance many factors, including whether a violation has occurred, likelihood of success, resource allocation, and overall agency policies
  • Courts lack manageable standards for reviewing such decisions because there is "no law to apply"
  • When an agency refuses to act, it does not exercise its coercive power over an individual and thus does not infringe upon protected rights

The presumption may be rebutted where the substantive statute provides guidelines for the agency to follow in exercising its enforcement powers, or where the agency has "consciously and expressly adopted a general policy" that is so extreme as to amount to an abdication of its statutory responsibilities.

Lincoln v. Vigil (1993)

508 U.S. 182

The Indian Health Service discontinued a program that provided specialized health care to disabled Indian children in the Southwest. Native American children and their parents challenged the agency's decision, arguing that it violated the APA and various Indian health care statutes.

The Supreme Court held that an agency's allocation of funds from a lump-sum appropriation is unreviewable under the APA because it is "committed to agency discretion by law." The Court also held that the decision to discontinue the program was not subject to notice-and-comment requirements because it was a general statement of policy.

Justice Souter's opinion explained that:

  • Lump-sum appropriations give agencies broad discretion to allocate funds among competing priorities
  • The allocation of funds from a lump-sum appropriation is a type of administrative decision that has traditionally been regarded as committed to agency discretion
  • Courts have no meaningful standard against which to judge an agency's exercise of discretion in allocating such funds

Block v. Community Nutrition Institute (1984)

467 U.S. 340

The Secretary of Agriculture issued milk market orders that set minimum prices for milk. Consumers challenged these orders, arguing they resulted in artificially high milk prices. The government contended that the Agricultural Marketing Agreement Act (AMAA) precluded judicial review of such challenges by consumers.

The Supreme Court held that the AMAA impliedly precluded consumers from obtaining judicial review of milk market orders. Although the APA creates a presumption of judicial review, this presumption can be overcome by inferences of congressional intent drawn from the statutory scheme as a whole.

Justice O'Connor's opinion:

  • Found that the AMAA's structure, purposes, and legislative history indicated that Congress intended to limit judicial review to suits by handlers (milk processors)
  • Noted that the statute provided a specific mechanism for handlers to challenge orders after exhausting administrative remedies
  • Reasoned that allowing consumer challenges would undermine the complex regulatory scheme designed by Congress
  • Emphasized that congressional intent to preclude review may be found in the statutory scheme even without express language

Bowen v. Michigan Academy of Family Physicians (1986)

476 U.S. 667

A group of physicians challenged a Medicare regulation that authorized different levels of payment for similar services depending on whether they were performed by MDs or osteopaths. The government argued that the Medicare statute barred judicial review of all payment determinations.

The Supreme Court held that the Medicare statute did not preclude judicial review of the regulation itself, distinguishing between review of individual benefit determinations and broader challenges to the validity of regulations. The Court emphasized the strong presumption in favor of judicial review of administrative action.

Justice Stevens' opinion:

  • Reaffirmed the presumption that Congress intends judicial review of administrative action
  • Held that this presumption can only be overcome by "clear and convincing evidence" of contrary legislative intent
  • Distinguished between challenges to the method of determining benefits (reviewable) and challenges to the amount of benefits awarded in individual cases (not reviewable)
  • Found no evidence that Congress intended to bar challenges to the validity of regulations themselves
  • Emphasized the importance of judicial review as a check on administrative action

Johnson v. Robison (1974)

415 U.S. 361

A conscientious objector who performed alternative civilian service was denied educational benefits under the Veterans' Readjustment Benefits Act, which limited benefits to those who had served on active duty. He challenged the constitutionality of this limitation under the First and Fifth Amendments. The government argued that judicial review was precluded by a statute stating that "no court shall have power or jurisdiction to review" decisions of the Veterans Administration.

The Supreme Court held that the statutory preclusion of review did not bar consideration of constitutional challenges to veterans' benefits legislation itself. The Court distinguished between review of individual benefits determinations and constitutional challenges to the underlying statute.

Justice Brennan's opinion:

  • Applied the principle of constitutional avoidance to interpret the preclusion provision narrowly
  • Distinguished between review of decisions of the Administrator (precluded) and review of decisions of Congress (not precluded)
  • Noted the serious constitutional questions that would arise if Congress attempted to bar judicial review of constitutional claims
  • Found no clear and convincing evidence that Congress intended to preclude judicial review of constitutional challenges to the statute itself

When Is Agency Action "Committed to Agency Discretion"?

Courts have identified several categories of agency actions that are typically unreviewable under the "committed to agency discretion" exception:

  • Enforcement Decisions: Agency decisions not to take enforcement action (Heckler)
  • Resource Allocation: Agency decisions allocating funds from lump-sum appropriations (Lincoln)
  • National Security: Certain actions involving foreign affairs, national security, or military matters
  • Personnel Decisions: Many employment or personnel decisions within federal agencies
  • No Law to Apply: Any action where there is "no law to apply" because the statute provides no meaningful standard against which to judge the agency's exercise of discretion

Standing: Who Can Challenge Agency Actions

Hello! I'm Standing Sam. I help courts determine who has the right to bring a case to federal court. My job is to ensure that only plaintiffs with a real stake in the outcome can challenge agency actions!

Standing is a constitutional and prudential doctrine that limits who can bring a lawsuit in federal court. To challenge an agency action, a plaintiff must satisfy both Article III's constitutional standing requirements and the "zone of interests" test under the APA.

APA § 702: Right of Review

A person suffering legal wrong because of agency action, or adversely affected or aggrieved by agency action within the meaning of a relevant statute, is entitled to judicial review thereof.

Massachusetts v. EPA (2007)

549 U.S. 497

A group of states, local governments, and private organizations challenged the EPA's refusal to regulate greenhouse gas emissions from new motor vehicles under the Clean Air Act. The EPA argued, among other things, that the petitioners lacked standing because global climate change was too generalized an injury and the requested regulation would not redress their injuries.

The Supreme Court held that Massachusetts had standing to challenge the EPA's refusal to regulate greenhouse gas emissions. The Court emphasized that states are entitled to "special solicitude" in the standing analysis when they protect their quasi-sovereign interests.

Justice Stevens' majority opinion found that the petitioners satisfied all three elements of Article III standing:

  • Injury: Massachusetts faced concrete injury from rising sea levels that could damage coastal property
  • Causation: EPA's refusal to regulate contributed to greenhouse gas emissions that cause global warming
  • Redressability: While regulation might not reverse global warming entirely, it would slow or reduce it, and courts do not require complete redress of an injury

The Court also emphasized states' special status as sovereign entities protecting their territory and citizens, which entitles them to "special solicitude" in the standing analysis.

Association of Data Processing Service Organizations v. Camp (1970)

397 U.S. 150

Data processing service organizations challenged a ruling by the Comptroller of the Currency that allowed national banks to offer data processing services to other banks and customers. The plaintiffs argued that this ruling violated the National Bank Act, which limited the business of national banks.

The Supreme Court held that the data processing service organizations had standing to challenge the ruling. The Court established a two-part test for standing under the APA: (1) whether the plaintiff suffered injury in fact, and (2) whether the interest sought to be protected is "arguably within the zone of interests to be protected or regulated by the statute or constitutional guarantee in question."

Justice Douglas's opinion:

  • Found that the plaintiffs suffered economic injury from the increased competition
  • Held that the "zone of interests" test requires that the interest sought to be protected be arguably within the zone of interests protected by the relevant statute
  • Determined that the National Bank Act was arguably intended to protect competitors of national banks
  • Distinguished between constitutional standing (injury in fact) and prudential standing (zone of interests)

Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife (1992)

504 U.S. 555

Environmental organizations challenged a rule interpreting the Endangered Species Act to apply only to actions within the United States or on the high seas. The plaintiffs argued they had standing because they had visited habitats of endangered species in the past and intended to do so again in the future.

The Supreme Court held that the plaintiffs lacked standing because they failed to show a "concrete and particularized" injury that was "actual or imminent." The Court rejected the "animal nexus" and "vocational nexus" theories of standing and emphasized that "some day" intentions to visit affected areas were insufficient to establish injury.

Justice Scalia's plurality opinion articulated a strict approach to standing:

  • The constitutional minimum of standing requires concrete injury, causation, and redressability
  • Procedural rights alone cannot satisfy Article III standing requirements
  • The "concrete injury" requirement cannot be eliminated by Congress through a "citizen-suit" provision
  • When challenging government regulation of a third party, the plaintiff faces a more difficult burden to establish standing

Clarke v. Securities Industry Association (1987)

479 U.S. 388

The Securities Industry Association (SIA), representing securities brokers and dealers, challenged a decision by the Comptroller of the Currency allowing national banks to establish discount brokerage offices across state lines. SIA argued this violated the National Bank Act and the McFadden Act, which limited interstate banking.

The Supreme Court held that SIA had standing to challenge the Comptroller's decision under the zone of interests test. The Court stated that this test is not demanding and is satisfied when the plaintiff's interests are "arguably within the zone of interests to be protected or regulated by the statute" in question.

Justice White's opinion:

  • Interpreted the zone of interests test broadly, rejecting a narrow approach that would limit standing to those who were the primary intended beneficiaries of a statute
  • Emphasized that the test "is not meant to be especially demanding" and should be applied with an "eye toward the obvious intent of the APA to make agency action presumptively reviewable"
  • Found that SIA's interest in limiting competition from banks was arguably within the zone of interests protected by the relevant banking laws
  • Noted that the laws were intended in part to protect certain competitors from undue bank competition

Air Courier Conference v. American Postal Workers Union (1991)

498 U.S. 517

The American Postal Workers Union challenged a regulation by the Postal Service that allowed private couriers to provide international remailing services, arguing it violated the Private Express Statutes (PES), which give the Postal Service a monopoly over mail delivery. The union argued that allowing competition would reduce revenue and potentially affect jobs.

The Supreme Court held that the union's interest in preserving jobs was not within the zone of interests protected by the PES. The Court emphasized that the zone of interests test requires that the plaintiff's interests fall within the zone of interests protected by the specific statutory provision being invoked, not just the statute as a whole.

Chief Justice Rehnquist's opinion:

  • Narrowed the zone of interests test by focusing on the specific statutory provision at issue, not the overall purpose of the statute
  • Found that the PES was enacted to protect the Postal Service's revenue, not to protect postal workers' jobs
  • Concluded that because the union's interest in preserving jobs was not within the zone of interests protected by the PES, it lacked standing to challenge the regulation
  • Emphasized the importance of identifying the specific statutory provision that forms the basis of the legal challenge

National Credit Union Administration v. First National Bank & Trust (1998)

522 U.S. 479

The National Credit Union Administration (NCUA) interpreted the Federal Credit Union Act, which limited credit union membership to "groups having a common bond of occupation or association," to allow credit unions to serve multiple unrelated employer groups. Banks and banking trade associations challenged this interpretation, arguing it would allow credit unions to expand in ways that would harm banks.

The Supreme Court held that the banks had prudential standing under the zone of interests test to challenge the NCUA's interpretation. The Court found that Congress, in limiting credit union membership, intended to prevent credit unions from competing with banks for customers outside the common bond limitation.

Justice Thomas's opinion:

  • Reaffirmed that the zone of interests test is not demanding and does not require the plaintiff to be an intended beneficiary of the statute
  • Found that banks were "suitable challengers" to the NCUA's interpretation because they were competitors who would be harmed by the expanded credit union membership
  • Determined that the common bond requirement was intended in part to limit the markets that credit unions could serve
  • Rejected the argument that only intended beneficiaries of a statute have standing to enforce it

Allen v. Wright (1984)

468 U.S. 737

Parents of Black children attending public schools filed a nationwide class action challenging the IRS's failure to deny tax-exempt status to private schools that discriminated on the basis of race. They claimed this government support for discriminatory schools interfered with their children's right to receive an education in desegregated public schools.

The Supreme Court held that the plaintiffs lacked standing because their injury (diminished ability to receive an education in a racially integrated school) was not fairly traceable to the challenged government action (the tax exemption for discriminatory private schools).

Justice O'Connor's opinion:

  • Found that the first claimed injury (stigmatic injury from government aid to discriminatory schools) was too abstract and generalized
  • Regarding the second claimed injury (impaired ability to attend integrated schools), concluded that the causal connection was too attenuated
  • Emphasized the importance of the causation requirement in ensuring that courts do not adjudicate abstract questions of wide public significance that are better addressed by the other branches
  • Explained that the line of causation between the tax exemptions and the segregated schools depended on the independent decisions of third parties (private schools and parents) not before the court

Simon v. Eastern Kentucky Welfare Rights Organization (1976)

426 U.S. 26

Low-income individuals and organizations representing them challenged an IRS ruling that allowed hospitals to maintain their tax-exempt status even if they provided only emergency room services to indigent patients. The plaintiffs claimed this ruling encouraged hospitals to deny them non-emergency services.

The Supreme Court held that the plaintiffs lacked standing because they failed to establish that their injury (denial of hospital services) was fairly traceable to the challenged action (the IRS ruling) or that the requested relief would likely redress their injury.

Justice Powell's opinion:

  • Found that the plaintiffs had not shown that hospitals would alter their policies and provide more services to indigents if the tax ruling were changed
  • Emphasized that it was "purely speculative" whether the hospital decisions were based on the tax ruling rather than other factors
  • Noted that hospitals might choose to forgo tax-exempt status rather than provide more charity care
  • Established that redressability is a key component of Article III standing: it must be "likely, as opposed to merely speculative, that the injury will be redressed by a favorable decision"

Clapper v. Amnesty International USA (2013)

568 U.S. 398

Attorneys, journalists, and human rights organizations challenged a provision of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) that authorized surveillance of non-U.S. persons abroad. The plaintiffs claimed they had to take costly measures to protect the confidentiality of their communications with foreign contacts who might be subject to surveillance.

The Supreme Court held that the plaintiffs lacked standing because their alleged injury (interception of their communications) was not "certainly impending" but merely speculative. The Court also rejected the argument that costs incurred to avoid potential surveillance constituted an injury-in-fact.

Justice Alito's majority opinion:

  • Emphasized that threatened injury must be "certainly impending" to constitute injury-in-fact, not merely possible or even probable
  • Found the plaintiffs' theory of standing relied on a "highly attenuated chain of possibilities"
  • Rejected the "reasonable likelihood" standard applied by the Second Circuit
  • Held that plaintiffs cannot "manufacture standing" by incurring costs in anticipation of non-imminent harm
  • Noted the special concerns in cases involving intelligence gathering and national security

FEC v. Akins (1998)

524 U.S. 11

A group of voters challenged the Federal Election Commission's determination that the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) was not a "political committee" subject to disclosure requirements under the Federal Election Campaign Act. The voters argued they were denied access to information about AIPAC's activities that would help them evaluate candidates for office.

The Supreme Court held that the voters had standing because they suffered an "informational injury" related to voting that was both concrete and particularized, despite being widely shared. The Court distinguished between a "generalized grievance" that is abstract and undifferentiated, and a concrete injury that happens to be widely shared.

Justice Breyer's opinion:

  • Established that a "widely shared" injury can still be sufficiently concrete and particularized to constitute injury-in-fact
  • Distinguished between "widely shared" injuries (potentially acceptable) and "abstract and indefinite" generalized grievances (unacceptable)
  • Found that the denial of information that voters could use in casting their votes was a concrete injury
  • Determined that the injury was fairly traceable to the FEC's decision and likely to be redressed by a favorable ruling
  • Recognized that Congress had intended to create a right to information and authorized citizens to enforce that right

Lexmark International, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc. (2014)

572 U.S. 118

Lexmark, a printer manufacturer, sued Static Control for copyright infringement. Static Control counterclaimed under the Lanham Act, alleging Lexmark engaged in false advertising by telling customers that Static Control's products violated Lexmark's intellectual property rights. Lexmark argued that Static Control lacked "prudential standing" because it did not fall within the "zone of interests" protected by the Lanham Act.

The Supreme Court held that the "zone of interests" test is not a matter of prudential standing but rather a question of statutory interpretation: whether the plaintiff "falls within the class of plaintiffs whom Congress has authorized to sue" under the statute. The Court found that Static Control's interests fell within the zone of interests protected by the Lanham Act.

Justice Scalia's unanimous opinion:

  • Recharacterized the zone of interests test as a matter of statutory interpretation rather than prudential standing
  • Explained that the test asks whether the plaintiff's interests "fall within the zone of interests protected by the law invoked"
  • Emphasized that Congress is presumed to "legislate against the background of" the zone of interests test
  • Applied the test to conclude that Static Control's interests fell within the zone of interests protected by the Lanham Act's false advertising provision

The Article III Standing Test

To have Article III standing, a plaintiff must establish three elements:

  1. Injury in Fact: The plaintiff must have suffered an "injury in fact" that is:
    • Concrete and particularized (not abstract or generalized)
    • Actual or imminent (not conjectural or hypothetical)
  2. Causation: The injury must be "fairly traceable" to the challenged action of the defendant, not the result of independent action of a third party not before the court
  3. Redressability: It must be "likely," as opposed to merely "speculative," that the injury will be redressed by a favorable decision

In addition to these constitutional requirements, in APA cases, the plaintiff must also show that the interest they seek to protect is "arguably within the zone of interests to be protected or regulated by the statute" that the agency action allegedly violated.

United States v. Texas (2023)

599 U.S. 577

The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) announced a policy (the "DAPA program") that would allow undocumented immigrants who had been in the country for five years and had citizen or lawful permanent resident children to apply for deferred action. Texas and other states challenged the program, arguing they had standing because of the financial costs associated with providing services to DAPA recipients.

The Supreme Court held that Texas and the other states had standing to challenge the DAPA program because they demonstrated a concrete and particularized injury (financial costs) that was fairly traceable to the challenged action and likely to be redressed by a favorable decision.

Justice Kavanaugh's majority opinion:

  • Found that the states' financial costs associated with issuing driver's licenses and providing other services to DAPA recipients constituted a concrete injury
  • Determined that the injury was fairly traceable to the DAPA policy
  • Concluded that vacating the DAPA policy would likely redress the states' injuries
  • Distinguished the case from Simon v. Eastern Kentucky Welfare Rights Organization, where the causal chain was too speculative

Ripeness: When Agency Actions Are Ready for Review

The ripeness doctrine ensures that courts review agency actions only when they are sufficiently concrete and when immediate review would not interfere with ongoing agency proceedings. It prevents courts from entangling themselves in abstract disagreements over administrative policies.

Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner (1967)

387 U.S. 136

The FDA issued regulations requiring that prescription drug labels and advertisements include the generic name of the drug each time the brand name appeared. Pharmaceutical companies challenged these regulations before they were enforced against any specific company.

In addition to establishing a presumption of reviewability, the Supreme Court articulated a two-prong test for ripeness:

  1. The fitness of the issues for judicial decision
  2. The hardship to the parties of withholding court consideration

The Court found that the challenge to the FDA regulations was ripe because the issue was purely legal, the regulations were final agency action, and the companies would face significant hardship if they had to wait until enforcement actions to challenge the regulations.

Justice Harlan's opinion explained that:

  • The challenge raised a purely legal question about agency authority
  • The regulations were "final agency action" under APA § 704
  • The regulations had a direct and immediate impact on the pharmaceutical companies' day-to-day operations
  • Companies faced a difficult choice: either comply with potentially invalid regulations at significant cost or risk serious penalties for non-compliance

Ohio Forestry Association, Inc. v. Sierra Club (1998)

523 U.S. 726

The Sierra Club challenged the U.S. Forest Service's land and resource management plan for the Wayne National Forest in Ohio. The plan allowed for logging in certain areas but did not authorize the cutting of any specific trees. Additional administrative steps and site-specific decisions would be required before any logging could occur.

The Supreme Court held that the challenge to the forest plan was not ripe for judicial review because the plan did not create any legal rights or obligations, did not force the Sierra Club to immediate action, and would not hinder the Sierra Club's ability to challenge specific logging decisions in the future.

Justice Breyer's opinion considered three factors to determine hardship:

  • Whether the challenged agency action created adverse effects of a strictly legal kind (legal force or practical effect)
  • Whether the action required an immediate change in the plaintiffs' conduct with serious penalties for noncompliance
  • Whether postponing review would cause significant hardship

The Court found that the forest plan itself did not inflict significant practical harm because it did not authorize any specific logging activities without further administrative action, and the Sierra Club could challenge those future site-specific decisions when they occurred.

The Abbott Laboratories Ripeness Test

Courts consider two factors when determining whether an agency action is ripe for review:

  1. Fitness of the Issues:
    • Is the issue purely legal, or does it require further factual development?
    • Is the agency action final?
    • Would judicial review interfere with further administrative action?
  2. Hardship to the Parties:
    • Would delayed review cause immediate and significant hardship?
    • Does the action have direct and immediate impact on daily business?
    • Does the action put the plaintiff in a difficult dilemma (e.g., comply at great cost or risk penalties)?

Finality: When Agency Actions Are Final

The APA authorizes judicial review of "final agency action." The finality requirement ensures that courts review only agency actions that represent the consummation of the agency's decision-making process and that determine rights or obligations or from which legal consequences will flow.

APA § 704: Actions Reviewable

Agency action made reviewable by statute and final agency action for which there is no other adequate remedy in a court are subject to judicial review.

Bennett v. Spear (1997)

520 U.S. 154

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service issued a "Biological Opinion" regarding a federal water project's impact on endangered species. The opinion concluded that the project would jeopardize certain species and proposed "reasonable and prudent alternatives" to protect the species while allowing the project to proceed. Two Oregon irrigation districts and ranchers challenged the opinion.

The Supreme Court articulated a two-prong test for determining whether an agency action is "final" under APA § 704:

  1. The action must mark the consummation of the agency's decision-making process—it must not be of a merely tentative or interlocutory nature
  2. The action must be one by which rights or obligations have been determined, or from which legal consequences will flow

The Court found that the Biological Opinion satisfied both prongs and thus was final agency action subject to judicial review.

Justice Scalia's opinion found that:

  • The Biological Opinion was the culmination of the Fish and Wildlife Service's consultation process
  • While the Bureau of Reclamation made the ultimate decision regarding the project, the Biological Opinion had direct and appreciable legal consequences
  • The Bureau of Reclamation would risk civil and criminal penalties if it disregarded the Biological Opinion's terms
  • The Biological Opinion effectively altered the legal regime to which the Bureau of Reclamation was subject

U.S. Army Corps of Engineers v. Hawkes Co. (2016)

578 U.S. 590

Hawkes Co., which mines peat for use in golf courses, sought to expand its mining operations. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers issued a jurisdictional determination (JD) stating that the property contained "waters of the United States" subject to Clean Water Act permitting requirements. Hawkes challenged the JD as a final agency action reviewable under the APA.

The Supreme Court held that the Corps' JD was a final agency action subject to judicial review under the APA because it satisfied both prongs of the Bennett test and there was no other adequate remedy available to Hawkes.

Chief Justice Roberts' unanimous opinion reasoned that:

  • The JD marked the consummation of the Corps' decision-making process on the question of jurisdiction
  • The JD had legal consequences—a negative JD would provide a five-year safe harbor from enforcement, while a positive JD effectively left landowners with only two options: proceed without a permit and risk enforcement action, or apply for a costly permit they believe is unnecessary
  • No other adequate remedy was available—pursuing a permit application or risking an enforcement action were not adequate alternatives to challenging the JD directly

Determining Finality Under Bennett

To be "final" agency action under the APA, the action must satisfy both prongs of the Bennett test:

  1. Consummation of Decision-Making: The action must represent the culmination of the agency's decision-making process, not a preliminary, procedural, or intermediate step
  2. Legal Consequences: The action must determine rights or obligations or result in legal consequences. This may be shown by:
    • Direct regulation of primary conduct
    • Creation of new legal rights or obligations
    • Changes to the legal framework governing regulated entities
    • Exposing parties to penalties or liability

Exhaustion of Administrative Remedies

The exhaustion doctrine requires parties to pursue all available administrative remedies before seeking judicial review. This requirement allows agencies to apply their expertise, develop a factual record, and potentially resolve disputes without court involvement.

Darby v. Cisneros (1993)

509 U.S. 137

The Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) imposed administrative sanctions on a real estate developer for violations of federal regulations. The developer sought judicial review without exhausting optional administrative appeals within HUD, and HUD argued that the case should be dismissed for failure to exhaust administrative remedies.

The Supreme Court held that when the APA applies, courts may not require exhaustion of administrative remedies beyond those required by statute or agency rules. Section 704 of the APA limits the exhaustion doctrine to situations where explicitly mandated by statute or agency rule.

Justice Blackmun's unanimous opinion:

  • Section 704 of the APA states that agency action is final "whether or not there has been presented or determined an application for... any form of reconsideration, or, unless the agency otherwise requires by rule and provides that the action meanwhile is inoperative, for an appeal to superior agency authority"
  • Courts may not impose additional exhaustion requirements beyond those provided by statute or agency rule
  • Where an agency rule requires administrative appeal but does not make the initial decision inoperative during the appeal, the initial decision is final and reviewable under the APA

McCarthy v. Madigan (1992)

503 U.S. 140

A federal prisoner filed a Bivens action seeking money damages for alleged deliberate indifference to his medical needs. The prisoner did not exhaust the Federal Bureau of Prisons' administrative remedy procedure, which could have provided various forms of relief but not the money damages he sought.

The Supreme Court held that the prisoner was not required to exhaust administrative remedies before filing his Bivens action because (1) the Bureau's administrative procedure did not provide for recovery of money damages, (2) meaningful administrative review was compromised by the agency's institutional bias, and (3) the prisoner would face an unreasonable burden in exhausting administrative procedures.

Justice Blackmun's opinion identified three circumstances where the interests of the individual weigh against requiring administrative exhaustion:

  • When requiring exhaustion would prejudice a subsequent court action (e.g., due to unreasonable delay or indefinite timeframe)
  • When the agency is biased or has predetermined the issue
  • When administrative remedies are inadequate (e.g., the agency lacks authority to grant effective relief)

Purposes of the Exhaustion Doctrine

The exhaustion requirement serves several important purposes:

  • Agency Expertise: Allows agencies to apply their specialized knowledge and experience to complex issues
  • Factual Development: Ensures development of a complete administrative record for judicial review
  • Agency Autonomy: Respects the agency's authority to correct its own errors
  • Efficiency: Promotes efficiency by avoiding premature judicial intervention
  • Judicial Economy: Conserves judicial resources by potentially eliminating the need for court review

Exceptions to the Exhaustion Requirement

Courts may waive exhaustion requirements in several circumstances:

  1. Futility: When exhaustion would be futile because the agency has predetermined the issue or lacks authority to provide relief
  2. Inadequate Remedies: When administrative remedies are inadequate to provide the relief sought (e.g., money damages unavailable)
  3. Irreparable Harm: When the plaintiff would suffer irreparable harm if required to pursue administrative remedies
  4. Constitutional Questions: When the challenge involves a constitutional question clearly beyond the agency's jurisdiction or expertise
  5. Bias: When the administrative body is biased or has prejudged the case
