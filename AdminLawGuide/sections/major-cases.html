This section provides detailed summaries of landmark administrative law cases, organized by doctrinal area. For each case, we include the key facts, holding, reasoning, and significance to administrative law.

Nondelegation Doctrine and Major Questions

A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States (1935)

295 U.S. 495

The National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA) authorized the President to approve "codes of fair competition" for various industries. These codes would have the force of law. The codes were to be developed by industry groups, subject to certain conditions, and then approved by the President. Schechter Poultry was prosecuted for violating the "Live Poultry Code."

The Supreme Court unanimously struck down the NIRA, holding that it unconstitutionally delegated legislative power to the President. The Court found that the Act provided no standards or rules of conduct to limit discretion, only broad policy objectives.

Chief Justice Hughes's opinion emphasized:

  • The Constitution does not permit Congress to abdicate its legislative functions by delegating unfettered discretion to the executive branch
  • While Congress may establish general standards and delegate rulemaking authority, it must provide an "intelligible principle" to guide the executive's discretion
  • The NIRA's authorization to create codes for "fair competition" was too vague and standardless
  • The delegation was particularly problematic because it allowed private industry groups to draft the codes
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Cardozo's concurrence (joined by Justice Stone):

  • Described the delegation as "delegation running riot" â€“ a phrase that has become famous in administrative law
  • Distinguished between delegation of power to regulate an industry within defined limits (acceptable) and this case, where the delegation had "no boundaries"
  • Noted that the NIRA gave the President "virtually unfettered" discretion to make whatever codes he thought appropriate
  • Emphasized that delegation is permissible only when there are standards that meaningfully constrain the delegated power

This concurrence is particularly influential because it colorfully articulated the core concern of the nondelegation doctrine and has been cited in subsequent cases to emphasize the need for meaningful constraints on delegated power.

Significance

Schechter Poultry represents one of only two cases in which the Supreme Court has struck down a statute on nondelegation grounds (the other being Panama Refining Co. v. Ryan). The case established the "intelligible principle" test for evaluating delegations, which remains the nominal test today, though the Court has since applied it very deferentially to uphold broad delegations.

Whitman v. American Trucking Associations (2001)

531 U.S. 457

The Clean Air Act required the EPA to set National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) at levels "requisite to protect the public health" with "an adequate margin of safety." Industry groups challenged this delegation as lacking an "intelligible principle."

The Supreme Court unanimously upheld the delegation, finding that the instruction to set standards "requisite to protect the public health" with "an adequate margin of safety" provided a sufficiently intelligible principle to guide the EPA's discretion.

Justice Scalia's opinion:

  • Reaffirmed that the Constitution limits Congress's ability to delegate legislative power
  • Held that the Clean Air Act's language was comparable to other delegations the Court had upheld
  • Rejected the D.C. Circuit's suggestion that the EPA could "cure" an unconstitutional delegation by adopting limiting constructions of its authority
  • Stated that the Court had "almost never felt qualified to second-guess Congress regarding the permissible degree of policy judgment that can be left to agencies"
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Thomas's concurrence:

  • Wrote separately to question whether the Court's approach to the nondelegation doctrine had strayed too far from the Constitution's original meaning
  • Suggested that the Court should reconsider its nondelegation precedents in a future case
  • Argued that the Court's increasingly permissive approach to delegation may violate Article I's vesting of "all legislative Powers" in Congress
  • Questioned whether the "intelligible principle" test adequately enforces the constitutional separation of powers

This concurrence is significant because it signaled a willingness to reconsider the Court's deferential approach to nondelegation challenges, a position that has gained support among several justices in more recent cases.

Significance

Whitman demonstrated the Court's continued reluctance to invalidate broad delegations under the nondelegation doctrine. It also established that agencies cannot "cure" an unconstitutional delegation through their own limiting constructions of a statute, emphasizing that the intelligible principle must come from Congress.

West Virginia v. EPA (2022)

142 S. Ct. 2587

The EPA issued the Clean Power Plan under Section 111(d) of the Clean Air Act, setting carbon dioxide emission limits for power plants that would effectively require a shift from coal to natural gas and renewable energy. The EPA claimed that the "best system of emission reduction" language in Section 111 authorized this approach.

The Supreme Court, in a 6-3 decision, invalidated the Clean Power Plan under the major questions doctrine, holding that Congress must speak clearly if it wishes to assign decisions of vast economic and political significance to an administrative agency.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • Formalized the "major questions doctrine" as a canon of statutory interpretation distinct from the nondelegation doctrine
  • Reasoned that the EPA's interpretation would transform Section 111 from a "backstop" to a program that could reshape the nation's energy sector
  • Emphasized that such a "transformative expansion" of authority required a clear statement from Congress
  • Noted that the EPA had never previously interpreted Section 111 to authorize generation shifting or setting energy policy
  • Pointed to Congress's repeated consideration and rejection of similar cap-and-trade programs
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Gorsuch's concurrence (joined by Justice Alito):

  • Provided a detailed framework for applying the major questions doctrine
  • Connected the doctrine to nondelegation principles and separation of powers
  • Outlined factors for determining when a question is "major," including economic impact, political significance, and intrusion into areas traditionally regulated by states
  • Emphasized that agencies cannot invoke "vague language" to claim authority over major questions

Justice Kagan's dissent (joined by Justices Breyer and Sotomayor):

  • Criticized the majority for preventing the EPA from doing what Congress authorized it to do: address carbon pollution
  • Argued that "best system of emission reduction" naturally encompasses generation shifting
  • Contended that the majority's approach prevents agencies from addressing new problems with existing statutory tools
  • Characterized the major questions doctrine as a "get-out-of-text-free card" for limiting agency authority
  • Warned that constraining the EPA's authority would hamper the government's ability to respond to climate change

These opinions are significant because they represent competing visions of agency power: the concurrence emphasizing strict limits on delegation for major questions, and the dissent advocating for agency flexibility to address evolving problems within broad statutory mandates.

Significance

West Virginia elevated the major questions doctrine from a background principle to a formal canon of statutory interpretation. It established that when agencies claim authority to decide questions of major economic or political significance, courts should be skeptical and require clear congressional authorization, especially when the claimed authority involves a transformative expansion of regulatory authority, intrudes into areas traditionally regulated by states, or addresses a long-recognized policy question that Congress has previously considered.

Political Control of Agencies

INS v. Chadha (1983)

462 U.S. 919

Jagdish Rai Chadha, an East Indian born in Kenya, remained in the United States after his student visa expired and faced deportation. The Attorney General suspended his deportation under a statute that allowed either house of Congress to veto such suspensions. The House of Representatives vetoed the suspension of Chadha's deportation.

The Supreme Court struck down the legislative veto provision as unconstitutional, holding that actions with legislative effect must satisfy the Constitution's requirements of bicameralism (passage by both houses) and presentment (presentation to the President for approval or veto).

Chief Justice Burger's majority opinion:

  • Characterized the House's veto as a legislative act because it altered Chadha's legal rights and had the purpose and effect of making policy
  • Found that the Constitution explicitly prescribes the procedures for legislative action: passage by both houses and presentment to the President
  • Rejected arguments that the legislative veto was an efficient or necessary tool for controlling agencies
  • Emphasized that convenience and efficiency cannot justify departure from constitutional procedures designed to protect against legislative overreaching
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Powell's concurrence:

  • Provided a narrower rationale, arguing that Congress impermissibly exercised judicial power by determining that Chadha did not meet the statutory criteria for suspension of deportation
  • Emphasized that the House's action was adjudicative in nature, resembling a court ruling on his individual case
  • Contended that the decision could have been limited to this separation of powers ground without resolving the broader bicameralism and presentment issues

Justice White's dissent:

  • Argued that the legislative veto was an important and necessary check on the extensive delegations of power to executive agencies
  • Warned that the majority's decision would invalidate nearly 200 statutory provisions and disrupt the functioning of modern government
  • Contended that the legislative veto was not a "legislative act" requiring bicameralism and presentment, but rather a condition on a delegation of power
  • Emphasized the practical importance of the legislative veto in allowing Congress to delegate broadly while maintaining oversight

Justice Rehnquist's dissent (with Justice White):

  • Focused on the severability issue, arguing that the legislative veto could not be separated from the delegation of authority to the Attorney General
  • Contended that Congress would not have delegated such broad authority without the legislative veto as a check

These opinions highlight competing views on congressional oversight and the balance of power between branches. Justice White's dissent particularly emphasized the practical challenges of administration in the modern administrative state.

Significance

Chadha invalidated hundreds of legislative veto provisions in federal statutes, significantly altering the balance of power between Congress and administrative agencies. It forced Congress to use more formal mechanisms to control agency action, such as specific statutory directives, appropriations limits, and oversight hearings. The decision underscored the importance of the Constitution's structural requirements for lawmaking.

Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (2020)

140 S. Ct. 2183

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) was created by the Dodd-Frank Act as an independent agency headed by a single Director who could only be removed by the President for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office." The CFPB issued a civil investigative demand to Seila Law, which challenged the agency's structure as unconstitutional.

The Supreme Court held that the CFPB's structure violated the separation of powers by vesting significant executive power in a single individual who was insulated from presidential control. The Court severed the removal provision from the rest of the statute, allowing the CFPB to continue operating but with a Director removable at will by the President.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • Reaffirmed that Article II vests executive power in the President, who must "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed"
  • Recognized only two exceptions to the President's removal power: (1) multimember expert agencies and (2) certain inferior officers with limited duties
  • Distinguished Humphrey's Executor, which upheld removal restrictions for FTC commissioners, emphasizing the difference between a multimember commission and a single agency head
  • Emphasized that concentrating power in a single individual with removal protection was historically unprecedented and incompatible with the structure of the Constitution
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Thomas's concurrence (joined by Justice Gorsuch):

  • Argued for a more sweeping position that removal protections for any executive officer are unconstitutional
  • Contended that Humphrey's Executor and subsequent cases should be overruled
  • Rejected the idea that even multi-member commissions could constitutionally be insulated from presidential control
  • Based his argument on an originalist interpretation of the "executive Power" vested in the President

Justice Kagan's dissent (joined by Justices Ginsburg, Breyer, and Sotomayor):

  • Defended independent agencies as constitutional and consistent with historical practice
  • Argued that removal restrictions help ensure expert, apolitical decision-making in certain domains
  • Contended that the distinction between single-director and multi-member agencies was not constitutionally significant
  • Warned that the majority's decision threatened the independence of many agencies and concentrated more power in the President
  • Emphasized the role of Congress in structuring the administrative state to serve the public interest

These opinions represent competing theories of executive power and presidential control. The concurrence advocates for a unitary executive with complete control over all executive officers, while the dissent defends congressional power to create independent agencies insulated from direct presidential control.

Significance

Seila Law significantly limited Congress's ability to create independent agencies headed by a single individual. It clarified the scope of Humphrey's Executor and reinforced presidential control over administrative agencies. The decision reflects the Roberts Court's concern with the accumulation of power in independent agencies and its willingness to enforce the unitary executive theory's vision of presidential control.

Buckley v. Valeo (1976)

424 U.S. 1

The Federal Election Campaign Act (FECA) of 1971, as amended in 1974, created the Federal Election Commission (FEC) with members appointed by both the President and congressional leaders. The FEC was given extensive rulemaking, enforcement, and adjudicatory powers to administer and enforce campaign finance regulations. Various political candidates and groups challenged the constitutionality of the commission's structure and powers.

The Supreme Court held that the appointment method for FEC members violated the Appointments Clause of the Constitution. The Court ruled that because the FEC exercised executive powers, its members were "Officers of the United States" who must be appointed in accordance with the Appointments Clauseâ€”either by the President with Senate confirmation, or, for inferior officers, by the President alone, the courts, or department heads.

The Court's per curiam opinion:

  • Found that the FEC's powers were executive in nature, including the authority to bring civil enforcement actions
  • Held that Congress could not appoint members to a commission exercising executive powers without violating separation of powers principles
  • Determined that the Appointments Clause provided the exclusive means for appointing "Officers of the United States"
  • Emphasized that congressional appointment power is limited to officers performing duties within the legislative branch itself
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice White's concurrence in part and dissent in part:

  • Agreed that some of the FEC's powers were executive and required appointment consistent with the Appointments Clause
  • Dissented from the majority's position that all FEC powers were executive in nature
  • Argued that the Commission's rulemaking, advisory opinion, and information-gathering functions could be validly exercised by congressional appointees
  • Proposed a functional approach to separation of powers rather than a rigid categorical approach

Justice Burger's concurrence:

  • Emphasized that Congress cannot exercise executive authority by retaining the power to appoint those who perform executive functions
  • Stressed that the Framers sought to prevent the accumulation of excessive power in any single branch
  • Underscored the importance of presidential accountability for the execution of laws

These opinions reflect different approaches to separation of powers: Justice White's more flexible, functional approach versus a stricter, formalist view held by the majority and Justice Burger. The debate over formalist versus functionalist approaches to separation of powers remains significant in administrative law.

Significance

Buckley established important limits on Congress's ability to structure administrative agencies and appoint their officers. It reinforced separation of powers principles by ensuring that those who exercise executive power must be appointed through constitutional means. The decision also highlighted the distinction between legislative and executive functions, clarifying that enforcement of the law is an executive function that cannot be performed by officers appointed by Congress.

Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (2010)

561 U.S. 477

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to regulate accounting firms that audit public companies. PCAOB members could only be removed by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) for good cause, and SEC Commissioners could only be removed by the President for good cause, creating two layers of tenure protection.

The Supreme Court held that the dual for-cause removal restrictions were unconstitutional because they interfered with the President's ability to ensure that the laws are faithfully executed. The Court severed the for-cause removal provision for PCAOB members, making them removable at will by the SEC.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • Found that the dual layer of for-cause removal protection contravened the Constitution's separation of powers
  • Emphasized that the President's ability to execute the laws is impaired when he cannot hold subordinates accountable
  • Reasoned that the dual layer of protection made PCAOB members neither accountable to the President nor subject to the public's control through the ballot box
  • Distinguished Humphrey's Executor, which involved only a single layer of removal protection
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Breyer's dissent (joined by Justices Stevens, Ginsburg, and Sotomayor):

  • Argued that the dual-layer structure did not significantly affect presidential power, since the President could still remove SEC Commissioners for cause
  • Emphasized that the PCAOB's functions required technical expertise and independence from political pressure
  • Advocated for a functional, rather than formal, approach to separation of powers questions
  • Warned that the Court's reasoning could threaten the independence of many officials within independent agencies
  • Pointed out that the majority's holding was limited to dual-layer tenure protection but could have broader implications

This dissent is significant because it represents a competing vision of how separation of powers principles should apply to modern administrative structures. Justice Breyer's functionalist approach emphasized practical governance considerations and the values served by insulating certain officials from political pressure, contrasting with the majority's more formalist approach focusing on clear lines of accountability to the President.

Significance

Free Enterprise Fund significantly limited Congress's ability to insulate administrative officials from presidential control by prohibiting multiple layers of removal protection. The decision strengthened the unitary executive theory and signaled the Court's increasing skepticism of independent agencies that lack direct presidential oversight. The case also foreshadowed the Court's later decision in Seila Law.

Rulemaking and Adjudication

Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Corp. v. NRDC (1978)

435 U.S. 519

The Atomic Energy Commission (AEC) conducted rulemaking on the environmental effects of the nuclear fuel cycle, using the notice-and-comment procedures prescribed by the APA. The D.C. Circuit held that these procedures were inadequate and suggested additional procedures the agency should have used, such as cross-examination of witnesses.

The Supreme Court unanimously reversed, holding that courts cannot impose procedural requirements beyond those specified in the APA. Agencies are free to grant additional procedural rights, but courts may not require them to do so.

Justice Rehnquist's opinion:

  • Emphasized that the APA establishes "the maximum procedural requirements" courts may impose on agencies
  • Stated that agencies should be "free to fashion their own rules of procedure" beyond the APA's requirements
  • Warned that permitting courts to impose additional procedures would make administrative proceedings "totally unpredictable"
  • Criticized the D.C. Circuit's approach as "Monday morning quarterbacking" and "judicial intervention run riot"
Concurrences & Dissents

This was a unanimous decision with no separate concurrences or dissents. However, Justice Rehnquist's colorful language, particularly his characterization of the D.C. Circuit's approach as "judicial intervention run riot," has become influential in administrative law jurisprudence. This echoes Justice Cardozo's equally famous phrase "delegation running riot" from Schechter Poultry, creating a parallel between excessive delegation by Congress and excessive intervention by courts.

The lack of dissent is significant considering the strong rebuke to the D.C. Circuit's activist approach, showing a rare consensus among the justices about the proper role of courts in reviewing agency procedures. The Court unanimously rejected the notion that courts could impose procedural requirements beyond those in the APA based on their own views of what procedures would be "best."

Significance

Vermont Yankee established a fundamental principle of administrative law: courts cannot impose procedural requirements on agencies beyond those in the APA or other statutes. The decision preserved agency flexibility and prevented courts from micromanaging agency procedures. Despite this limitation on procedural review, courts later developed "hard look" review to scrutinize the substantive reasonableness of agency decisions.

NLRB v. Bell Aerospace Co. (1974)

416 U.S. 267

The National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) reversed its long-standing position that buyers were "managerial employees" excluded from the National Labor Relations Act's protection. Bell Aerospace argued that this policy change required rulemaking rather than adjudication.

The Supreme Court held that the NLRB had discretion to establish new policy through adjudication rather than rulemaking, even when overturning longstanding precedent. The Court stated that "the choice between rulemaking and adjudication lies primarily in the informed discretion of the administrative agency."

Justice Powell's opinion:

  • Noted that the NLRB has traditionally developed policy through case-by-case adjudication
  • Recognized that adjudication allows agencies to address problems that were not anticipated when rules were created
  • Emphasized that agencies need flexibility to choose between rulemaking and adjudication
  • Acknowledged that retroactive application of new policies through adjudication might be inappropriate in some cases
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Douglas's concurrence in part and dissent in part:

  • Agreed with the Court's holding that the NLRB could develop policy through adjudication
  • Dissented from the Court's conclusion that buyers at Lackawanna plant were "managerial employees" exempt from NLRA protection
  • Argued that this determination should be made by the NLRB on remand rather than by the Court
  • Emphasized the NLRB's expertise in determining employee classifications

This partial dissent highlights the tension between judicial review and agency expertise. While Justice Douglas supported agency flexibility in procedural choices, he also advocated for greater deference to the NLRB's substantive determinations about which employees fall within the NLRA's protection, a position consistent with his generally pro-labor views.

Significance

Bell Aerospace established that agencies have broad discretion to choose between rulemaking and adjudication as means of policymaking. This discretion remains a fundamental feature of administrative law, though courts have occasionally suggested that rulemaking might be more appropriate for major policy changes. The decision reflects the pragmatic recognition that agencies need flexibility to address unforeseen circumstances and evolving problems.

United States v. Nova Scotia Food Products Corp. (2d Cir. 1977)

568 F.2d 240

The FDA issued regulations establishing time, temperature, and salinity requirements for processing smoked fish to prevent botulism. Nova Scotia Food Products Corp., which produced smoked whitefish, argued that the regulations would destroy the commercial viability of their product. The FDA failed to disclose scientific research upon which the regulations were based and did not respond to the company's comments and proposed alternative methods.

The Second Circuit invalidated the regulations, holding that the FDA violated the APA's notice-and-comment requirements by failing to disclose scientific data underlying the proposed rule and failing to respond to significant comments and proposed alternatives.

Judge Gurfein's opinion:

  • Emphasized that meaningful public participation in rulemaking requires disclosure of scientific data upon which the agency relies
  • Held that agencies must respond to significant comments, particularly when they suggest less burdensome alternatives
  • Found that the FDA's failure to consider species-specific regulations was arbitrary when evidence showed that different fish species required different processing methods
  • Noted that a satisfactory explanation for agency action must include rational consideration of all relevant factors
Significance

Nova Scotia established important procedural requirements for informal rulemaking under the APA. It held that agencies must disclose the technical studies and data upon which they rely and must respond to significant comments, including proposed alternatives. The decision enhanced the transparency of the rulemaking process and strengthened the requirement that agencies engage meaningfully with stakeholder input, particularly when presented with less burdensome regulatory alternatives.

United States v. Mead Corp. (2001)

533 U.S. 218

The U.S. Customs Service issued a tariff classification ruling letter that reclassified Mead's day planners as "bound diaries" subject to tariff. These ruling letters were not the product of notice-and-comment rulemaking and were not intended to have precedential effect.

The Supreme Court held that Chevron deference applies only when Congress has delegated authority to the agency to make rules carrying the force of law, and the agency interpretation was promulgated in the exercise of that authority. For less formal interpretations, courts may still give them respect according to their persuasiveness (Skidmore deference).

Justice Souter's opinion:

  • Established a preliminary inquiry (later called "Chevron Step Zero") about whether Chevron applies at all
  • Identified indicators of congressional intent to delegate force-of-law authority, including authorization for rulemaking or adjudication
  • Found that tariff classification ruling letters did not qualify for Chevron deference because they were informal, numerous, and not intended to have the force of law
  • Revived Skidmore deference for agency interpretations that don't qualify for Chevron deference
Concurrences & Dissents

Justice Scalia's dissent:

  • Criticized the majority for creating an "avulsive change" in judicial review of agency action
  • Argued that Chevron should apply to all authoritative agency interpretations of statutes they administer
  • Contended that the majority's approach would create confusion and unpredictability, as courts would have to determine what level of deference to apply in each case
  • Warned that the revival of Skidmore would lead to "totality of the circumstances" judicial decision-making with unpredictable results
  • Characterized the majority's approach as "th'ol' 'totality of the circumstances' test" that would lead to "uncertainty, unpredictability, and endless litigation"

Justice Scalia's dissent has been particularly influential because it accurately predicted the confusion that would follow in determining when Chevron applies. His critique of Mead as creating an unpredictable standard has been cited by scholars and judges who advocate for either a broader application of Chevron or, more recently, for abandoning the Chevron framework entirely, as eventually occurred in Loper Bright.

Significance

Mead significantly limited the scope of Chevron deference by establishing that it applies only to agency interpretations that carry the force of law. The decision created a more nuanced approach to judicial review, with Chevron deference for formal interpretations and Skidmore deference for less formal ones. Mead also incentivized agencies to use more formal procedures to secure greater judicial deference. (Note: This framework was subsequently overruled by Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024).)

Judicial Review of Agency Action

Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. (1983)

463 U.S. 29

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) rescinded a rule requiring passive restraints (automatic seatbelts or airbags) in new cars. The agency cited doubts about safety benefits and costs, particularly because it believed most manufacturers would install detachable automatic seatbelts that might not be used.

The Supreme Court held that NHTSA's rescission of the passive restraint rule was arbitrary and capricious because the agency failed to consider obvious alternatives and did not offer a reasoned explanation for its decision.

Justice White's majority opinion:

  • Established that the standard for rescinding a rule is the same as for promulgating one: the agency must examine relevant data and articulate a satisfactory explanation
  • Outlined factors that make agency action arbitrary and capricious, including failing to consider important aspects of the problem
  • Found that NHTSA failed to consider requiring airbags as an alternative and failed to explain why automatic seatbelts would not increase safety
  • Rejected the suggestion that a change in presidential administrations justified a more lenient standard of review
Significance

State Farm established the framework for "hard look" review of agency actions under the arbitrary and capricious standard. It requires agencies to provide reasoned explanations based on relevant factors and to consider obvious alternatives. The decision has been enormously influential in shaping judicial review of agency actions, ensuring that agencies engage in reasoned decision-making even as policy priorities change between administrations.

Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc. (1984)

467 U.S. 837

The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) promulgated regulations interpreting the term "stationary source" in the Clean Air Act to include an entire industrial plant (the "bubble concept") rather than individual pieces of equipment. This allowed plants to modify some equipment without triggering stringent permit requirements as long as total emissions did not increase.

The Supreme Court established a two-step framework for reviewing agency interpretations of statutes: (1) If Congress has directly spoken to the precise question at issue, courts must follow that clear intent; (2) If the statute is ambiguous, courts should defer to any reasonable agency interpretation.

Justice Stevens's opinion:

  • Found that the Clean Air Act did not explicitly define "stationary source" or address the bubble concept
  • Determined that the EPA's interpretation was reasonable and represented a permissible construction of the statute
  • Explained that agencies may adopt different interpretations over time based on evolving circumstances and priorities
  • Recognized agencies' expertise and political accountability as justifications for judicial deference
Significance

Chevron became one of the most cited cases in administrative law, establishing a framework for judicial deference to agency statutory interpretations. For nearly four decades, it significantly shaped the relationship between courts and agencies, generally giving agencies more leeway to interpret ambiguous statutes. However, Chevron was ultimately overruled by Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo in 2024.

Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024)

No. 22-451, 600 U.S. ___ (2024)

Fishing companies challenged a rule issued by the National Marine Fisheries Service that required them to pay for federal observers on their vessels. The rule was based on the Magnuson-Stevens Act, which required fishers to allow observers aboard but did not explicitly address who should pay for them.

The Supreme Court explicitly overruled Chevron, holding that courts should no longer defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes. Instead, courts must exercise their independent judgment to determine the best reading of the statute, while giving due respect to agency expertise and considered views.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • Found that Chevron was inconsistent with the APA's instruction that "the reviewing court shall decide all relevant questions of law"
  • Held that judicial deference to agency interpretations violated separation of powers principles and abdicated the judicial duty to say what the law is
  • Criticized Chevron for leading to instability and unpredictability in the law, with statutes' meanings changing with each administration
  • Acknowledged that while courts should give appropriate respect to agency views, they must not "defer" by elevating the agency's interpretation over the court's best reading of the statute
Significance

Loper Bright represents a seismic shift in administrative law by overruling the longstanding Chevron doctrine. The decision fundamentally alters the relationship between courts and agencies, giving courts more authority to interpret statutes and potentially limiting agency flexibility. It reflects the Court's growing skepticism of the administrative state and preference for more robust judicial review of agency actions.

Kisor v. Wilkie (2019)

139 S. Ct. 2400

A Vietnam War veteran sought retroactive disability benefits based on his PTSD diagnosis. The Department of Veterans Affairs denied his request, interpreting its regulation to require new "relevant" records, which it determined did not include the veteran's psychiatric records.

The Supreme Court declined to overrule Auer deference but significantly narrowed its application. Justice Kagan's opinion established a multi-step test for when courts should defer to an agency's interpretation of its own regulations.

Justice Kagan's majority opinion:

  • Preserved Auer deference but limited it to cases where: (1) the regulation is genuinely ambiguous; (2) the agency's interpretation is reasonable; (3) the interpretation represents the agency's authoritative position; (4) the interpretation implicates the agency's substantive expertise; and (5) the interpretation reflects the agency's fair and considered judgment
  • Emphasized that before deferring, courts must exhaust all traditional tools of interpretation
  • Instructed courts not to defer to "convenient litigating positions" or "post hoc rationalizations"

Justice Gorsuch's concurrence argued that Auer deference should be overruled entirely as inconsistent with the APA and separation of powers principles.

Significance

Kisor preserved but significantly narrowed Auer deference to agency interpretations of their own regulations. The decision reflects a compromise between those who wanted to maintain some form of deference and those who wanted to eliminate it entirely. After Loper Bright's overruling of Chevron, it remains to be seen whether Auer/Kisor deference will survive, as many of the same criticisms apply to both doctrines.

Access to Judicial Review

Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner (1967)

387 U.S. 136

The FDA issued regulations requiring that prescription drug labels and advertisements include the generic name of the drug each time the brand name appeared. Pharmaceutical companies challenged these regulations before they were enforced against any specific company.

The Supreme Court established a presumption in favor of judicial review of agency actions and articulated a two-part test for ripeness: (1) the fitness of the issues for judicial decision, and (2) the hardship to the parties of withholding court consideration.

Justice Harlan's opinion:

  • Interpreted the APA's "generous review provisions" as embodying a presumption of reviewability
  • Found that pre-enforcement review was appropriate because the challenge raised a purely legal question about agency authority
  • Emphasized that the regulations had a direct and immediate impact on the pharmaceutical companies, who faced a difficult choice between compliance and risking enforcement
  • Rejected the government's argument that judicial review should be postponed until enforcement proceedings
Significance

Abbott Laboratories established key principles of judicial review that remain foundational today: a presumption of reviewability and a framework for determining when pre-enforcement challenges are ripe. The decision significantly expanded access to judicial review by allowing regulated entities to challenge regulations before they are enforced, rather than waiting for enforcement actions and risking penalties.

Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife (1992)

504 U.S. 555

Environmental organizations challenged a rule interpreting the Endangered Species Act to apply only to actions within the United States or on the high seas. The plaintiffs argued they had standing because they had visited habitats of endangered species in the past and intended to do so again in the future.

The Supreme Court held that the plaintiffs lacked standing because they failed to show a "concrete and particularized" injury that was "actual or imminent." The Court rejected "animal nexus" and "vocational nexus" theories of standing and emphasized that "some day" intentions were insufficient to establish injury.

Justice Scalia's plurality opinion:

  • Articulated a strict three-part test for Article III standing: injury in fact, causation, and redressability
  • Found that the plaintiffs' assertions of injury were too speculative to satisfy the "actual or imminent" requirement
  • Held that procedural rights alone cannot satisfy Article III standing requirements
  • Emphasized that the "concrete injury" requirement cannot be eliminated by Congress through a "citizen-suit" provision
Significance

Lujan represents a significant restriction on environmental standing and citizen suits. Justice Scalia's opinion established a more rigorous approach to standing requirements than prior cases, making it more difficult for plaintiffs to challenge agency actions, especially those with diffuse or future impacts. The decision particularly affected environmental groups by requiring them to demonstrate concrete, imminent injury to specific members rather than relying on more generalized environmental concerns.

Massachusetts v. EPA (2007)

549 U.S. 497

A group of states, local governments, and private organizations challenged the EPA's refusal to regulate greenhouse gas emissions from new motor vehicles under the Clean Air Act. The EPA argued that greenhouse gases were not "air pollutants" under the Act and that even if they were, the agency had discretion not to regulate them due to scientific uncertainty and policy considerations.

The Supreme Court held that states have "special solicitude" in the standing analysis and that Massachusetts had standing to challenge the EPA's refusal to regulate greenhouse gases. On the merits, the Court held that greenhouse gases were "air pollutants" under the Clean Air Act, and the EPA could decline to regulate them only based on factors specified in the statute.

Justice Stevens's majority opinion:

  • Found that states have "special solicitude" in the standing analysis because of their quasi-sovereign interests in protecting their territory
  • Determined that Massachusetts had demonstrated injury through rising sea levels that threatened coastal property, causation through EPA's failure to regulate emissions that contribute to climate change, and redressability because regulation would slow the pace of global emissions
  • Held that the Clean Air Act's definition of "air pollutant" unambiguously included greenhouse gases
  • Rejected the EPA's policy reasons for declining to regulate, holding that the agency must base its decision on the statutory standard of whether greenhouse gases "cause, or contribute to, air pollution which may reasonably be anticipated to endanger public health or welfare"
Significance

Massachusetts significantly expanded standing for states in environmental cases and limited agency discretion to decline regulation based on factors not specified in the relevant statute. The decision led directly to the EPA's subsequent regulation of greenhouse gases and established that agencies cannot evade statutory mandates by invoking policy considerations outside the statutory framework. The case also represents a more flexible approach to standing than Lujan, especially for states protecting quasi-sovereign interests.

Heckler v. Chaney (1985)

470 U.S. 821

Death row inmates challenged the Food and Drug Administration's (FDA) refusal to take enforcement action against states using drugs for lethal injections, arguing that these uses violated the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA). The FDA declined to investigate, claiming discretion over its enforcement decisions.

The Supreme Court held that agency decisions not to take enforcement actions are presumptively unreviewable under APA Â§ 701(a)(2) as actions "committed to agency discretion by law," because they involve balancing factors that are peculiarly within the agency's expertise.

Justice Rehnquist's opinion:

  • Drew an analogy between agency enforcement decisions and prosecutorial discretion, which has traditionally been unreviewable
  • Emphasized that agencies must balance many factors in making enforcement decisions, including resource allocation, likelihood of success, and fit with the agency's overall policies
  • Noted that when an agency refuses to act, it does not exercise its coercive power over individuals, so there is less need for judicial review to protect individual rights
  • Established that the presumption against review may be rebutted where the statute provides guidelines for the agency to follow in exercising its enforcement powers or where the agency has "consciously and expressly adopted a general policy" that amounts to an abdication of its statutory responsibilities
Significance

Heckler established a significant limitation on judicial review of agency inaction, creating a presumption that decisions not to enforce are unreviewable. This presumption has been extended to other forms of agency inaction and discretionary decisions. The decision respects agency resource allocation decisions and prevents courts from micromanaging agency enforcement priorities, though it allows review in cases where agencies abandon statutory responsibilities or violate clear statutory directives.
