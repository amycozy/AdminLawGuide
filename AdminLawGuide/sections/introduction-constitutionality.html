The Administrative State and Its Constitutional Foundations

The modern administrative state consists of numerous agencies that perform vital governmental functions. These agencies operate at the intersection of the three traditional branches of government, combining aspects of legislative, executive, and judicial power. This structure raises fundamental questions about the proper allocation of governmental authority under the Constitution.

What Do Agencies Do?

Administrative agencies serve several critical functions:

  • Rulemaking: Creating regulations with the force of law to implement statutory directives
  • Adjudication: Resolving disputes and enforcing compliance with regulations
  • Licensing and Permitting: Granting permissions for regulated activities
  • Investigation and Enforcement: Monitoring compliance and penalizing violations
  • Information Gathering and Dissemination: Collecting data and issuing guidance

Rationales for Administrative Agencies

Several justifications support the delegation of authority to administrative agencies:

  • Market Failures: Addressing externalities, information asymmetries, monopolies, and public goods
  • Expertise: Leveraging specialized knowledge for complex problems
  • Efficiency: Responding quickly and adapting to changing conditions
  • Collective Values: Protecting resources and providing services based on shared societal values
  • Redistribution: Implementing policies to increase equality and protect vulnerable populations

The case of Massachusetts v. EPA (2007) illustrates these principles in action, where the Supreme Court considered the EPA's authority and obligation to regulate greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act.

The Nondelegation Doctrine

The nondelegation doctrine stems from Article I of the Constitution, which states that "All legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested in a Congress." This raises a fundamental question: Can Congress delegate its legislative powers to the executive branch (including administrative agencies)?

A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States (1935)

295 U.S. 495

The National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA) authorized the President to approve "codes of fair competition" written by various industries. These codes would then have the force of law. Schechter Poultry was charged with violating the "Live Poultry Code."

The Supreme Court unanimously struck down this delegation as unconstitutional, with Justice Cardozo referring to it as "delegation running riot." The Court held that Congress must provide an "intelligible principle" to guide agency discretion.

The Court found the delegation problematic because:

  • It effectively allowed industry groups to write laws governing themselves
  • The term "fair competition" was too vague and undefined
  • There were insufficient constraints on the President's discretion
  • This amounted to Congress abdication of its lawmaking function

Whitman v. American Trucking Associations (2001)

531 U.S. 457

The Clean Air Act directed the EPA to set air quality standards "requisite to protect the public health" with "an adequate margin of safety." Industry groups challenged this delegation as lacking an "intelligible principle."

The Supreme Court upheld the delegation, finding that the Clean Air Act provided a sufficiently intelligible principle to guide the EPA's discretion.

Justice Scalia, writing for the Court, explained:

  • The Court has "almost never felt qualified to second-guess Congress regarding the permissible degree of policy judgment that can be left to agencies"
  • The phrase "requisite to protect the public health" with "an adequate margin of safety" provides sufficient guidance
  • The Court has only twice in its history found statutes that lacked an intelligible principle
  • An agency cannot cure an unconstitutionally broad delegation by adopting limiting constructions of the statute

Gundy v. United States (2019)

139 S. Ct. 2116

The Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA) gave the Attorney General authority to "specify the applicability" of registration requirements to pre-Act offenders.

In a fragmented 5-3 decision (with Justice Kavanaugh not participating), the Court upheld the delegation. However, the dissent and separate opinions signaled potential willingness to revive a stronger nondelegation doctrine.

Justice Kagan's plurality opinion found that SORNA's delegation was permissible because:

  • The statute conveyed Congress's policy that the Attorney General should require pre-Act offenders to register as soon as feasible
  • The Attorney General's authority was limited to addressing transitional issues

Justice Gorsuch's dissent argued that the nondelegation doctrine should be reinvigorated, allowing delegation only when:

  • Congress makes policy decisions and leaves executive to "fill up the details"
  • Congress makes policy decisions and leaves executive to engage in fact-finding
  • There is overlapping congressional and executive authority (e.g., foreign affairs)

Justice Alito's concurrence expressed willingness to reconsider the Court's approach to nondelegation if a majority were ready to do so.

Note: The Supreme Court has granted certiorari in FCC v. Consumers' Research and SHLB Coalition v. Consumers' Research, consolidated cases that may provide an opportunity for the Court to revisit the nondelegation doctrine. These cases challenge the FCC's authority to assess fees and delegate certain functions to private entities.

Three Views of Nondelegation

  1. Strict View: The vesting of all legislative powers in Congress is a final allocation from which there can be no further delegation. (Justice Thomas's position)
  2. Moderate View: Congress can delegate unless the scope of the grant is too broad, requiring line-drawing. (Current majority approach)
  3. Permissive View: When Congress enacts a statute granting authority to the executive, it exercises its legislative power rather than delegating it. (Justice Stevens's position)

The Major Questions Doctrine

Hello! I'm Major Questions Mole. I help courts dig deep into statutory interpretation when agencies claim authority over questions of vast economic and political significance. I demand clear congressional authorization for major agency actions!

The Major Questions Doctrine (MQD) is a relatively recent development in administrative law, though its roots can be traced to earlier cases. It holds that courts should not defer to an agency's interpretation of a statute on questions of major economic or political significance unless Congress has clearly authorized the agency to address those questions.

Core Principle of MQD

"We expect Congress to speak clearly if it wishes to assign to an executive agency decisions of vast economic and political significance." - West Virginia v. EPA (2022)

West Virginia v. EPA (2022)

142 S. Ct. 2587

The EPA promulgated the Clean Power Plan, which established emission guidelines for power plants and required shifts from coal to natural gas and renewable energy sources. The EPA claimed authority under Section 111 of the Clean Air Act.

The Supreme Court invalidated the Clean Power Plan under the major questions doctrine, finding that the EPA's assertion of authority to restructure the nation's electricity production constituted a "transformative expansion" of its regulatory authority without clear congressional authorization.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • Section 111 had never before been used to regulate beyond specific sources
  • The EPA's interpretation would give it unprecedented authority over a significant portion of the American economy
  • Congress had previously considered and rejected similar cap-and-trade programs
  • The statutory language fell "far short of the clear congressional authorization" required

Biden v. Nebraska (2023)

143 S. Ct. 2355

The Secretary of Education sought to forgive federal student loan debt for approximately 43 million borrowers at an estimated cost of $430 billion. The Secretary relied on the HEROES Act, which allows the Secretary to "waive or modify" federal student loan provisions during national emergencies.

The Supreme Court invalidated the loan forgiveness program under the major questions doctrine, finding that the Secretary's claimed authority to cancel $430 billion of student loan principal required clear congressional authorization.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • The HEROES Act's authority to "waive or modify" provisions does not naturally encompass the power to cancel debt
  • The Secretary's reading would transform a statute designed for emergencies into the basis for a fundamental change in student loan policy
  • The economic and political significance of the action ($430 billion) required clear congressional authorization
  • Congress had previously authorized more limited loan forgiveness programs in specific circumstances

When Does the Major Questions Doctrine Apply?

According to Justice Gorsuch's concurrence in West Virginia v. EPA, the doctrine is likely to apply when:

  1. An agency claims power to resolve a matter of great political significance or end a profound national debate
  2. An agency seeks to regulate a significant portion of the economy or requires significant financial expenditure
  3. An agency seeks to intrude into an area traditionally reserved to the states

To identify clear congressional authority, courts should consider:

  1. The statutory provisions in their larger context (their place in the overall legislative scheme)
  2. The age and focus of the statute in relation to the problem the agency seeks to address
  3. The agency's past interpretations of the relevant statute
  4. Whether there's a mismatch between the agency's action and its congressionally assigned mission and expertise

Congressional Control of Agencies

Congress exercises control over administrative agencies through various mechanisms, some of which raise constitutional concerns related to separation of powers.

INS v. Chadha (1983)

462 U.S. 919

The Immigration and Nationality Act allowed either house of Congress to veto the Attorney General's decision to suspend deportation. When the House vetoed the AG's suspension of Chadha's deportation, he challenged the constitutionality of this "legislative veto."

The Supreme Court struck down the legislative veto as unconstitutional, holding that congressional actions that are legislative in nature must satisfy the requirements of bicameralism (passage by both houses) and presentment (submission to the President).

Chief Justice Burger's majority opinion:

  • The Constitution prescribes specific procedures for lawmaking in Article I
  • The House's action was legislative in character because it altered the legal rights, duties, and relations of persons outside the legislative branch
  • The Constitution carefully constrains the legislative process with bicameralism and presentment requirements
  • These constraints serve important purposes: careful deliberation, representation, and checks and balances

Constitutional Tools of Congressional Control

  • Appropriations: Congress controls agency funding through appropriations powers
  • Oversight Hearings: Congress monitors agencies through committee hearings
  • Advice and Consent: Senate confirms or rejects presidential nominees to key agency positions
  • Statutory Specificity: Congress can limit agency discretion through detailed legislation
  • Congressional Review Act (CRA): Allows Congress to disapprove new agency rules through joint resolutions subject to presidential veto

The Congressional Review Act

The Congressional Review Act (1996) provides a mechanism for Congress to review and potentially disapprove new federal regulations before they take effect. Key features include:

  • Agencies must submit all rules to Congress before they can take effect
  • Congress has 60 legislative days to pass a joint resolution of disapproval
  • If the resolution passes and is signed by the President (or Congress overrides a veto), the rule cannot take effect
  • An agency may not reissue a rule in "substantially the same form" unless specifically authorized by a subsequent law

The CRA was rarely used until 2017, when it was employed to overturn 16 rules from the Obama administration. It continues to be used during presidential transitions when control of the White House changes parties.

Presidential Control of Agencies

The President exercises control over administrative agencies through various mechanisms derived from Article II of the Constitution, which vests executive power in the President and requires that the President "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed."

Competing Views on Presidential Authority

  • Unitary Executive Theory: The President has comprehensive control over the executive branch, including the power to direct agency decisions and remove officials at will
  • Oversight Model: The President's role is to oversee agencies and ensure faithful execution of the laws, but not to dictate substantive decisions when Congress has delegated authority to agencies

Tools of Presidential Control

  • Executive Orders: Presidential directives that manage operations of the executive branch
  • Appointment Power: President nominates high-level agency officials (with Senate confirmation)
  • Removal Power: President can remove certain agency officials
  • Centralized Regulatory Review: Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (OIRA) reviews significant agency rules
  • Budget Process: Presidential influence through OMB budget preparation
  • Directive Authority: Direct instructions to agencies (scope contested)

OIRA Review

The Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (OIRA), located within the Office of Management and Budget (OMB), conducts centralized review of significant regulatory actions. Key features include:

  • Executive Order 12,866 (1993) requires executive agencies to submit significant regulatory actions to OIRA for review
  • Agencies must conduct cost-benefit analysis for major rules
  • OIRA reviews ensure consistency with presidential priorities
  • Independent agencies are encouraged, but not required, to submit rules for OIRA review
  • OIRA review has been criticized for causing delay and prioritizing economic concerns over health and safety

Appointment and Removal Powers

The Constitution's Appointments Clause (Article II, ยง 2) governs how officers of the United States are appointed, while the President's power to remove officers has been developed through Supreme Court doctrine.

Buckley v. Valeo (1976)

424 U.S. 1

The Federal Election Campaign Act created a Federal Election Commission with six voting members: two appointed by the President, two by the President pro tempore of the Senate, and two by the Speaker of the House.

The Supreme Court held that this appointment structure violated the Appointments Clause because FEC commissioners exercised "significant authority" and were therefore "Officers of the United States" who must be appointed by the President with Senate consent or, if "inferior officers," as provided by Congress.

The Court held:

  • An "Officer of the United States" is "any appointee exercising significant authority pursuant to the laws of the United States"
  • FEC commissioners exercised significant authority through rulemaking, advisory opinions, and enforcement actions
  • The Appointments Clause provides the exclusive means for appointing Officers of the United States
  • Congress cannot appoint Officers of the United States

Myers v. United States (1926)

272 U.S. 52

A statute required Senate consent for the President to remove certain postmasters. President Wilson removed Myers without Senate consent, and Myers sued for back pay.

The Supreme Court held that the President has exclusive authority to remove executive officers whom he has appointed with Senate confirmation, and Congress cannot limit this power.

Chief Justice Taft's opinion reasoned:

  • The executive power is vested in the President, who must "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed"
  • These responsibilities require the power to select and remove executive officials
  • Congressional limitation on removal would undermine the separation of powers
  • Historical practice supported the President's removal power

Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935)

295 U.S. 602

A statute limited the President's power to remove Federal Trade Commission members to specified causes ("inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office"). President Roosevelt removed Humphrey without claiming any of these causes.

The Supreme Court limited Myers and upheld the removal restriction, holding that Congress could limit the President's power to remove officials of independent agencies that perform quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions.

The Court distinguished between:

  • "Purely executive" officers, whom the President may remove at will
  • Officials of independent agencies with quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions, for whom Congress may impose removal restrictions

The Court emphasized the FTC's independence from executive control and its need for expertise and continuity.

Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (2020)

140 S. Ct. 2183

The Dodd-Frank Act created the CFPB with a single director removable by the President only for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office." Seila Law challenged this structure as unconstitutional.

The Supreme Court held that the CFPB's single-director structure with removal protection violated the separation of powers. The Court recognized only two exceptions to the President's unrestricted removal power: (1) multimember expert agencies and (2) inferior officers with limited duties.

Chief Justice Roberts's majority opinion:

  • The Constitution vests executive power in the President, who must "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed"
  • The President's ability to remove executive officials is central to this power
  • The CFPB's structure concentrated significant executive power in a single individual who is insulated from presidential control
  • This structure had no historical precedent and was incompatible with the structure of the Constitution
  • The removal restriction was severable from the rest of the statute

Current Framework for Removal Restrictions

  1. The President generally has unrestricted power to remove executive officers
  2. Exceptions where Congress may impose "for cause" removal restrictions:
    • Multimember expert agencies with quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial functions
    • Inferior officers with limited duties
  3. Impermissible removal restrictions:
    • Single-director agencies with significant executive authority
    • Multiple layers of "for cause" protection

Lucia v. Securities & Exchange Commission (2018)

138 S. Ct. 2044

The SEC brought an administrative proceeding against Raymond Lucia for violating securities laws. The case was heard by an SEC administrative law judge (ALJ) who was not appointed by the SEC Commissioners but by SEC staff. Lucia challenged the ALJ's authority, arguing that ALJs were "Officers of the United States" who must be appointed in accordance with the Appointments Clause.

The Supreme Court held that SEC ALJs are "Officers of the United States" subject to the Appointments Clause because they exercise significant authority and have career appointments. Therefore, they must be appointed by the President, courts, or department heads.

Justice Kagan's majority opinion:

  • Applied Buckley v. Valeo and Freytag v. Commissioner to determine that SEC ALJs are officers, not mere employees
  • Found that SEC ALJs exercise significant discretion in carrying out important functions (taking testimony, conducting trials, ruling on evidence, and issuing decisions)
  • Noted that ALJs have significant authority and independent discretion in presiding over adversarial proceedings
  • Concluded that the SEC's ALJs were appointed in violation of the Constitution because they were selected by staff rather than the Commission itself (the "head of department")

Edmond v. United States (1997)

520 U.S. 651

Coast Guard officers were convicted by courts-martial and their convictions were affirmed by the Coast Guard Court of Criminal Appeals. The judges on this court were appointed by the Secretary of Transportation, not the President. The officers challenged these appointments as violating the Appointments Clause.

The Supreme Court held that the judges on the Coast Guard Court of Criminal Appeals were "inferior officers" rather than "principal officers," and thus their appointment by the Secretary of Transportation (a department head) was constitutional.

Justice Scalia's opinion established a test for distinguishing between principal and inferior officers:

  • "[I]nferior officers" are "officers whose work is directed and supervised at some level by others who were appointed by Presidential nomination with the advice and consent of the Senate"
  • Coast Guard judges were inferior officers because their decisions were reviewable by the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces, and they could be removed by the Judge Advocate General
  • The key inquiry is whether the officer has a superior other than the President who directs and supervises their work
  • The fact that an officer performs important duties with significant discretion does not make them a principal officer if they are supervised by principal officers
