Memory Aids for Key Doctrines

Administrative law contains many complex doctrines with multi-factor tests. These memory aids will help you recall the essential elements of each doctrine quickly.

MARS: Major Questions Analysis

Remember the key factors in Major Questions Doctrine analysis with the acronym "MARS":

  • Major economic or political significance
  • Authorization clarity from Congress
  • Regulatory history of the agency
  • Statutory context and purpose

Example: In West Virginia v. EPA, the Court examined whether the Clean Air Act's general provision to regulate air pollutants clearly authorized the EPA's sweeping regulations of power plant emissions. The Court found the economic and political significance too great for such general statutory language.

PAPER: Arbitrary & Capricious Review

Remember the State Farm factors for "hard look" review with the acronym "PAPER":

  • Pertinent data examination
  • Articulated explanation
  • Problem aspects consideration
  • Evaluating alternatives
  • Reasoned change justification

Example: In Department of Homeland Security v. Regents of the University of California, the Court invalidated the rescission of DACA because DHS failed to consider important aspects of the problem, including reliance interests of DACA recipients.

CRISP: Standing Requirements

Remember the elements of standing with the acronym "CRISP":

  • Concrete injury
  • Real and particularized harm
  • Imminent or actual
  • Sourced from defendant (causation)
  • Possible to redress

Example: In Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, the Court rejected standing because plaintiffs' possible future visits to areas affected by challenged actions were too speculative to constitute an "imminent" injury.

FINAL: Finality Doctrine

Remember Bennett v. Spear's two-part test for final agency action with "FINAL":

  • Full conclusion of decision-making
  • Impact that is direct
  • Now affecting rights or obligations
  • Authority exercised with force of law
  • Legal consequences flowing from action

Example: In U.S. Army Corps of Engineers v. Hawkes Co., the Court found that a jurisdictional determination under the Clean Water Act was final agency action because it marked the consummation of the agency's decision-making process and had legal consequences.

Doctrine Relationships

Understanding how different administrative law doctrines relate to each other is crucial for comprehensive analysis. These visualizations show the historical development and conceptual relationships of major doctrines.

Deference Doctrine Evolution

Skidmore v. Swift (1944)

Established respect for agency interpretations based on their "power to persuade" - thoroughness, validity, consistency, expertise

Chevron v. NRDC (1984)

Two-step framework: (1) Is statute clear? (2) If ambiguous, is agency interpretation reasonable?

Auer/Seminole Rock (1997)

Deference to agency interpretations of their own regulations

Mead Corp. (2001)

Limited Chevron to cases where Congress delegated authority to act with force of law

Kisor v. Wilkie (2019)

Narrowed Auer deference with new limitations and prerequisites

Loper Bright (2024)

Overturned Chevron, returning to traditional tools of statutory interpretation

Arbitrary & Capricious Review Factors

Under Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm, a court will find agency action arbitrary and capricious if the agency:

  1. Failed to examine relevant data
  2. Failed to articulate a satisfactory explanation
  3. Failed to consider an important aspect of the problem
  4. Offered an explanation that runs counter to the evidence
  5. Offered an explanation so implausible it can't be attributed to expertise
  6. Relied on factors Congress did not intend the agency to consider
  7. Changed course without acknowledging the change or giving good reasons (including reliance interests)

Judicial Review Prerequisites

A.C.T.I.O.N. Framework for Judicial Review

Remember all the prerequisites for judicial review with this acronym:

  • Agency action (identifiable)
  • Cause of action (APA or other)
  • Timely (statute of limitations, ripeness)
  • Interests injured (standing)
  • Other remedies exhausted
  • Not precluded (by statute or committed to agency discretion)

If any element is missing, the court cannot proceed to the merits of the case.
