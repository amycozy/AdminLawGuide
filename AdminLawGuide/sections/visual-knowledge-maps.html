Doctrinal Timelines

These timelines trace the evolution of key administrative law doctrines through important Supreme Court cases, helping you understand how the field has developed over time.

Presidential Control Timeline

Myers v. United States (1926)

President has unrestricted power to remove executive officers he has appointed with Senate confirmation.

Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935)

Congress can limit President's removal power over commissioners of independent agencies who perform quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial functions.

Wiener v. United States (1958)

President could not remove a member of the War Claims Commission despite no explicit removal restrictions in statute.

Morrison v. Olson (1988)

Upheld independent counsel statute with "good cause" removal restriction, focusing on whether restriction impermissibly interferes with President's duties.

Free Enterprise Fund v. PCAOB (2010)

Struck down dual for-cause removal restrictions as unconstitutionally limiting presidential control.

Seila Law v. CFPB (2020)

Struck down single-director structure with removal protection, distinguishing Humphrey's as applying only to multi-member commissions.

This timeline shows the evolution of presidential removal power doctrine, illustrating the pendulum swings between greater presidential control and agency independence throughout the Court's history.

Administrative Rulemaking Timeline

Administrative Procedure Act (1946)

Established the basic framework for agency rulemaking, including notice-and-comment procedures in § 553.

United States v. Florida East Coast Railway (1973)

Limited formal rulemaking requirements, holding that "hearing" language alone does not trigger §§ 556-557 procedures.

Vermont Yankee v. NRDC (1978)

Courts cannot impose procedural requirements beyond those in the APA.

Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. v. State Farm (1983)

Established "hard look" review of agency rulemaking under the arbitrary and capricious standard.

Long Island Care at Home v. Coke (2007)

Recognized the "logical outgrowth" doctrine, requiring final rules to be logically connected to proposed rules.

Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Association (2015)

Agencies can change interpretive rules without notice and comment, overturning the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine.

This timeline traces key developments in administrative rulemaking procedure, showing the evolution from the APA's enactment through significant judicial interpretations that have shaped modern rulemaking practices.
