Doctrine Mascots: Visual Memory Aids

Administrative law contains complex doctrines that can be difficult to remember and apply. These visual mascots represent key doctrines in a memorable way, helping you build mental models for understanding and recalling important principles.

Doctrine Mascot Gallery

Each mascot embodies the core principles of its doctrine. Use these visual representations to help you remember the key aspects of each legal test or principle.

The Chevron Chameleon

The Chevron Chameleon represents how courts adapt to different statutory interpretations under the Chevron doctrine. Just as a chameleon changes its appearance to blend with its environment, courts adjust their approach based on statutory clarity:

  • Step One: Has Congress directly spoken to the precise question at issue? If the intent of Congress is clear, that is the end of the matter.
  • Step Two: If the statute is ambiguous, is the agency's interpretation reasonable?
  • Post-Loper Bright: The Supreme Court has now rejected the Chevron framework, requiring courts to determine the best reading of the statute using traditional tools of interpretation.

The Major Questions Mole

The Major Questions Mole represents how courts scrutinize agency authority when agencies claim power to decide issues of vast economic and political significance:

  • Clear Statement Rule: For questions of major economic and political significance, agencies must point to clear congressional authorization.
  • Extraordinary Cases: Courts are skeptical when agencies claim to discover in long-extant statutes an unheralded power to regulate a significant portion of the economy.
  • Key Factor: Has Congress considered and rejected similar proposals?

Standing Sam

Standing Sam helps you remember the requirements for Article III standing, which determines who can bring cases to federal court:

  • Injury in Fact: Concrete, particularized, and actual or imminent harm
  • Causation: The injury must be fairly traceable to the challenged action
  • Redressability: It must be likely that a favorable decision will remedy the injury
  • Zone of Interests: The plaintiff must fall within the zone of interests protected by the statute

Branch Icons: Visual Categorization System

Throughout this guide, headings and sections are color-coded and marked with icons that represent the three branches of government. This visual system helps you quickly identify which branch of government is primarily involved in a particular doctrine or concept.

Legislative Branch

Sections marked with the legislative icon (🏛️) and green color theme involve Congress, statutory interpretation, or the delegation of legislative power. Key topics include:

  • Nondelegation Doctrine
  • Legislative Veto

Executive Branch

Sections marked with the executive icon (📋) and blue color theme involve the President, administrative agencies, or agency procedures. Key topics include:

  • Appointment and Removal Powers
  • Rulemaking Procedures

  • Executive Orders and Presidential Control

Judicial Branch

Sections marked with the judicial icon (⚖️) and purple color theme involve courts, judicial review, or standards of review. Key topics include:

  • Arbitrary and Capricious Review
  • Statutory Interpretation
  • Standing and Justiciability
  • Remedies in Administrative Law

Case Visualizations: Remembering Complex Cases

Key cases in administrative law often involve complex fact patterns and reasoning. These visual representations help you remember the essential facts and holdings of major cases.

West Virginia v. EPA (2022)

In West Virginia v. EPA, the Supreme Court invoked the Major Questions Doctrine to strike down the EPA's Clean Power Plan. The Court held that when an agency claims to discover in a long-extant statute an unheralded power to regulate a significant portion of the economy, it must point to clear congressional authorization.
