Doctrine Mascots: Visual Memory Aids

Administrative law contains complex doctrines that can be difficult to remember and apply. These visual mascots represent key doctrines in a memorable way, helping you build mental models for understanding and recalling important principles.

Meet Chevron Chameleon

Your guide to understanding judicial deference to agency interpretations. Just as chameleons adapt to their environment, courts under the Chevron doctrine would adapt their approach based on whether a statute is clear or ambiguous.

This friendly chameleon will help you navigate the complex world of statutory interpretation and agency deference!

Doctrine Mascot Gallery

Each mascot embodies the core principles of its doctrine. Use these visual representations to help you remember the key aspects of each legal test or principle.

The Chevron Chameleon

Hello! I'm Chevron Chameleon. When there's ambiguity in a statute, I used to help judges determine when to defer to an agency's reasonable interpretation. However, after the Loper Bright decision in 2024, the Supreme Court rejected the Chevron framework!

When confronted with an agency's interpretation of a statute, I would help courts apply a two-step analysis:

  1. Step One: Has Congress directly spoken to the precise question at issue? If the intent of Congress is clear, that is the end of the matter.
  2. Step Two: If the statute is ambiguous, is the agency's interpretation reasonable?

But remember, after Loper Bright Fisheries v. Raimondo (2024), courts no longer follow this framework and instead determine the best interpretation of the statute using traditional tools of statutory construction.

Chevron Checkpoint

When analyzing an agency's interpretation of a statute, ask yourself:

  • What does the statutory text actually say?
  • What are the traditional tools of statutory interpretation telling us?
  • How has the historical understanding of this provision evolved?

The Major Questions Mole

Hello! I'm Major Questions Mole. I help courts dig deep into statutory interpretation when agencies claim authority over questions of vast economic and political significance. I demand clear congressional authorization for major agency actions!

When examining agency authority over major questions, I look for:

  • Clear Statement Rule: For questions of major economic and political significance, agencies must point to clear congressional authorization.
  • Extraordinary Cases: Courts are skeptical when agencies claim to discover in long-extant statutes an unheralded power to regulate a significant portion of the economy.
  • Key Factor: Has Congress considered and rejected similar proposals?

Major Questions Checkpoint

When analyzing an agency's claim of authority, ask:

  • Is this a question of vast economic or political significance?
  • Has Congress provided clear authorization for the agency's action?
  • Is the agency attempting to resolve a major policy question of great societal importance?

The APAlligator

Hello! I'm the APAlligator. I help navigate the complex procedural waters of administrative rulemaking. I ensure agencies follow proper procedures when creating, amending, or repealing rules under the Administrative Procedure Act!

When examining agency rulemaking procedures, I focus on these key requirements:

  • Notice of Proposed Rulemaking: Agencies must publish proposed rules in the Federal Register
  • Comment Period: The public must have an opportunity to submit comments
  • Statement of Basis and Purpose: Final rules must include a concise general statement of their basis and purpose
  • 30-Day Delayed Effective Date: Substantive rules require at least 30 days before becoming effective

APA Checkpoint

When analyzing an agency's rulemaking process, ask:

  • Did the agency provide adequate notice of the proposed rule?
  • Did the agency consider and respond to significant comments?
  • Is the final rule a logical outgrowth of the proposed rule?
  • Does the rule fall under any APA exceptions (interpretive rules, policy statements, etc.)?

Standing Sam

Hello! I'm Standing Sam. I help courts determine who has the right to bring a case to federal court. My job is to ensure that only plaintiffs with a real stake in the outcome can challenge agency actions!

I help you remember the requirements for Article III standing, which determines who can bring cases to federal court:

  • Injury in Fact: Concrete, particularized, and actual or imminent harm
  • Causation: The injury must be fairly traceable to the challenged action
  • Redressability: It must be likely that a favorable decision will remedy the injury
  • Zone of Interests: The plaintiff must fall within the zone of interests protected by the statute

Standing Checkpoint

When analyzing whether a plaintiff has standing, ask:

  • Has the plaintiff suffered a concrete, particularized injury?
  • Is the injury fairly traceable to the defendant's conduct?
  • Will a favorable court decision likely redress the injury?
