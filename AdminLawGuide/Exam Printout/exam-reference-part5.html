ADMINISTRATIVE LAW EXAM REFERENCE - APA DEEP DIVE

Administrative Law Exam Reference - APA Deep Dive & Flowcharts

How to Use This Section: This part contains a detailed analysis of key Administrative Procedure Act provisions and flowcharts for analyzing administrative law problems. Use these frameworks to work methodically through complex questions on your exam.

I. APA Key Sections

A. APA § 551: Key Definitions

§ 551(4) "rule" - agency statement of general or particular applicability and future effect designed to implement, interpret, or prescribe law or policy... § 551(5) "rule making" - agency process for formulating, amending, or repealing a rule § 551(6) "order" - the whole or a part of a final disposition... of an agency in a matter other than rule making § 551(7) "adjudication" - agency process for the formulation of an order § 551(13) "agency action" - includes the whole or a part of an agency rule, order, license, sanction, relief, or the equivalent or denial thereof, or failure to act

Practical Implications of Definitions:

  • Whether an agency action is "rulemaking" or "adjudication" determines which procedures apply
  • "Agency action" definition limits what can be challenged under APA
  • Broad definition of "rule" includes legislative rules, interpretive rules, policy statements, and procedural rules

B. APA § 553: Rulemaking

§ 553(b) General notice of proposed rule making shall be published in the Federal Register... (1) a statement of the time, place, and nature of public rule making proceedings; (2) reference to the legal authority under which the rule is proposed; and (3) either the terms or substance of the proposed rule or a description of the subjects and issues involved. § 553(c) ...the agency shall give interested persons an opportunity to participate in the rule making through submission of written data, views, or arguments... the agency shall incorporate in the rules adopted a concise general statement of their basis and purpose. § 553(d) The required publication or service of a substantive rule shall be made not less than 30 days before its effective date...

Exceptions to § 553 Notice-and-Comment Requirements:

§ 553(a) This section applies... except to the extent that there is involved— (1) a military or foreign affairs function of the United States; or (2) a matter relating to agency management or personnel or to public property, loans, grants, benefits, or contracts. § 553(b) Except when notice or hearing is required by statute, this subsection does not apply— (A) to interpretative rules, general statements of policy, or rules of agency organization, procedure, or practice; or (B) when the agency for good cause finds... that notice and public procedure thereon are impracticable, unnecessary, or contrary to the public interest.

Flowchart: Is Notice-and-Comment Required?

  1. Is it a "rule" under § 551(4)? If no, § 553 doesn't apply.
  2. Is it expressly excluded under § 553(a) (military/foreign affairs or agency management/property/grants/contracts)? If yes, exempt.
  3. Is it an interpretive rule, general policy statement, or procedural rule under § 553(b)(A)? If yes, exempt.
  4. Does the agency have "good cause" under § 553(b)(B) to find notice and comment impracticable/unnecessary/contrary to public interest? If yes, exempt.
  5. Otherwise, notice-and-comment is required.

C. APA §§ 554, 556, 557: Formal Proceedings

§ 554(a) This section applies... in every case of adjudication required by statute to be determined on the record after opportunity for an agency hearing... § 556(d) A party is entitled to present his case or defense by oral or documentary evidence, to submit rebuttal evidence, and to conduct such cross-examination as may be required for a full and true disclosure of the facts... § 557(c) ...The record shall show the ruling on each finding, conclusion, or exception presented. All decisions... shall include a statement of— (A) findings and conclusions, and the reasons or basis therefor, on all the material issues of fact, law, or discretion presented on the record; and (B) the appropriate rule, order, sanction, relief, or denial thereof.

Trigger for Formal Procedures:

  • Formal rulemaking: Statute requires rules "on the record after opportunity for an agency hearing" (rarely required)
  • Formal adjudication: Statute requires determination "on the record after opportunity for an agency hearing" (more common)
  • Language must explicitly require "on the record" hearing (Florida East Coast Railway)

D. APA §§ 701-706: Judicial Review

§ 701(a) This chapter applies... except to the extent that— (1) statutes preclude judicial review; or (2) agency action is committed to agency discretion by law. § 702 A person suffering legal wrong because of agency action, or adversely affected or aggrieved by agency action within the meaning of a relevant statute, is entitled to judicial review thereof... § 704 Agency action made reviewable by statute and final agency action for which there is no other adequate remedy in a court are subject to judicial review... § 706 ...The reviewing court shall— (1) compel agency action unlawfully withheld or unreasonably delayed; and (2) hold unlawful and set aside agency action, findings, and conclusions found to be— (A) arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law; (B) contrary to constitutional right, power, privilege, or immunity; (C) in excess of statutory jurisdiction, authority, or limitations, or short of statutory right; (D) without observance of procedure required by law; (E) unsupported by substantial evidence in a case subject to sections 556 and 557... or otherwise reviewed on the record of an agency hearing provided by statute; or (F) unwarranted by the facts to the extent that the facts are subject to trial de novo by the reviewing court.

Flowchart: Is Judicial Review Available?

  1. Is the challenge within statute of limitations? (6 years unless specified elsewhere)
  2. Does a specific statute provide or preclude review? If precludes, stop - no review.
  3. Is the action "committed to agency discretion by law" under § 701(a)(2)? If yes, presumptively unreviewable.
  4. Is it "agency action" under § 551(13)? If no, not reviewable.
  5. Is it "final agency action" under § 704 (see Bennett test)? If no, not reviewable.
  6. Does plaintiff have standing? (Lujan test of injury, causation, redressability)
  7. Has plaintiff exhausted administrative remedies? If not, may be premature.
  8. Is the issue ripe? (Abbott Labs test of fitness and hardship)
  9. If all above satisfied, judicial review is available.

II. Flowcharts for Agency Action Analysis

A. Distinguishing Rule Types

Flowchart: Is this a Legislative Rule (requiring notice and comment)?

Legal Effect Test:

  1. Does the rule create new rights, duties, or obligations? If yes, likely legislative.
  2. Does the rule have binding legal effect on regulated parties? If yes, likely legislative.
  3. Does the rule bind the agency itself, removing discretion in future cases? If yes, likely legislative.
  4. Does the agency cite specific statutory authority for the rule? If yes, suggests legislative.
  5. Does the agency intend the rule to have the force of law? If yes, likely legislative.

Counter-Indicators (suggests interpretive rule, policy statement, or procedural rule):

  1. Does the rule merely explain existing statutory or regulatory requirements? If yes, likely interpretive.
  2. Does the rule leave agency free to exercise discretion in individual cases? If yes, likely policy statement.
  3. Does the rule address agency internal operations or interactions with public? If yes, likely procedural.
  4. Does the rule create only tentative guidance, not binding norms? If yes, likely policy statement.

B. Arbitrary & Capricious Review Analysis

Flowchart: Arbitrary & Capricious Analysis (State Farm)

Step 1: Identify Agency Action Type

  • New rule/policy implementation
  • Change in existing policy (Fox Television considerations)
  • Rescission of prior policy (State Farm & DHS v. Regents)
  • Refusal to initiate rulemaking/enforcement (Heckler presumption)

Step 2: Apply State Farm Factors

  1. Did the agency examine relevant data?
  2. Did the agency articulate a rational connection between facts and choice?
  3. Did the agency consider all important aspects of the problem?
  4. Is the agency's explanation consistent with evidence?
  5. Did the agency rely on factors Congress did not intend it to consider?

Step 3: For Policy Changes, Add Fox/Regents Analysis

  1. Did the agency acknowledge it is changing position?
  2. Did the agency provide good reasons for the change?
  3. If change rests on factual findings contradicting prior policy, did agency provide reasoned explanation?
  4. Did the agency consider serious reliance interests created by prior policy?
  5. Did the agency consider alternatives to complete rescission?

C. Exhaustion of Administrative Remedies

Flowchart: Is Exhaustion Required?

Step 1: Identify Type of Exhaustion

  1. Does statute explicitly require exhaustion? If yes, mandatory exhaustion applies.
  2. Is exhaustion required by agency regulation that was subjected to notice and comment? If yes, treated similar to statutory exhaustion.
  3. If neither, discretionary/prudential exhaustion doctrine applies (more exceptions).

Step 2: Check for Exceptions (mainly for prudential exhaustion)

  1. Futility: Would pursuit of administrative remedies be clearly useless?
  2. Irreparable Harm: Would requiring exhaustion cause irreparable injury?
  3. Inadequate Remedy: Is administrative remedy inadequate or unavailable?
  4. Bias/Prejudgment: Has agency demonstrated bias or prejudgment?
  5. Constitutional Question: Is claim a constitutional challenge outside agency expertise?
  6. Purely Legal Question: Is issue purely legal, not requiring agency fact-finding?

Step 3: Determine Effect of Non-Exhaustion

  • Statutory exhaustion: Jurisdictional bar if not exhausted (absent statutory exception)
  • Prudential exhaustion: Court may excuse for policy reasons

III. APA § 553 Exemption Analysis

A. Interpretive Rules

Definition: Rules that clarify or explain existing law rather than creating new legal obligations

Key Characteristics:

  1. Derived from and explains existing statutes or regulations
  2. No independent legal effect
  3. Persuasive but not binding on courts
  4. Agency can change without notice and comment (Perez)

Test to Identify:

  1. Does it create new rights/duties or merely explain existing ones?
  2. Does it derive from and stay within bounds of statute or regulation?
  3. Could the rule be upheld only as interpretation of existing law?
  4. Does the agency have power to enforce independent of rule?
  5. What did agency say about rule's nature and intended effect?

Judicial Deference: Receives Skidmore respect based on persuasiveness, not Chevron deference

B. General Statements of Policy

Definition: Statements indicating how agency intends to exercise discretionary power in future cases

Key Characteristics:

  1. Announces tentative intentions for future
  2. No binding effect on regulated parties
  3. Agency retains discretion to deviate in individual cases
  4. Prospective in nature

Test to Identify:

  1. Is there genuine agency discretion to deviate from policy in individual cases?
  2. Is declaratory language absent (e.g., "shall," "must," "will")?
  3. Is the statement framed as tentative, not final?
  4. Does it use language of likelihood rather than certainty?
  5. Do agency personnel maintain flexibility in application?

Examples: Enforcement priorities, non-binding guidance documents, agency manuals

C. Procedural Rules

Definition: Rules governing agency organization, procedure, or practice

Key Characteristics:

  1. Addresses internal agency operations or interactions with public
  2. Doesn't alter substantive standards by which agency evaluates applications
  3. Doesn't significantly affect substantive rights or interests

Test to Identify:

  1. Does rule affect primarily agency's internal operations?
  2. Does rule encode agency's organization, procedures for conducting business?
  3. Does rule merely alter manner in which parties present case to agency?
  4. Does rule have minimal substantive impact on regulated parties?

D.C. Circuit's "Substantial Impact" Test: A rule with substantial impact on regulated parties' rights or interests may not qualify for procedural exception, even if procedural in form

D. Good Cause Exception

Definition: APA § 553(b)(B) exception when notice and comment would be "impracticable, unnecessary, or contrary to public interest"

Key Requirements:

  1. Agency must explicitly invoke the exception
  2. Agency must provide reasoned explanation why exception applies
  3. Courts interpret narrowly as emergency provision

"Impracticable" Situations:

  • Genuine emergency requiring immediate action
  • Public health/safety crisis requiring swift response
  • Natural disaster response
  • Statutory deadline impossible to meet with notice-and-comment

"Unnecessary" Situations:

  • Minor technical amendments with minimal effect
  • Mathematical corrections
  • Rules updating references or addresses

"Contrary to Public Interest" Situations:

  • Advance notice would defeat purpose of rule (e.g., price controls)
  • Advance notice would enable regulated parties to circumvent regulation
  • National security concerns
