ADMINISTRATIVE LAW EXAM REFERENCE - CROSS-DOCTRINAL ANALYSIS

Administrative Law Exam Reference - Cross-Doctrinal Analysis & Practice Tips

How to Use This Section: This part contains advanced analysis showing how administrative law doctrines interact with each other, along with practical tips for issue spotting and avoiding common exam mistakes. Use these insights to develop sophisticated responses to complex fact patterns.

I. Cross-Doctrinal Interactions

A. Separation of Powers & Statutory Interpretation

Key Interaction: Constitutional separation of powers concerns often drive statutory interpretation approaches.

Major Doctrinal Connections:

  1. Nondelegation + Major Questions: Both reflect concern about Congress delegating important policy decisions. Major Questions Doctrine can be viewed as nondelegation-lite, enforcing constitutional concerns through statutory interpretation.
  2. Removal Power + Agency Independence: Presidential removal restrictions reflect tension between independence and accountability. Courts interpret statutory silence to preserve presidential removal power.
  3. Article III Power + Agency Adjudication: Courts narrowly construe agency adjudicatory authority to avoid Article III concerns, especially for private rights.
  4. Appointment Power + Agency Structure: Courts scrutinize statutes creating innovative agency structures to ensure Appointments Clause compliance.

Practical Consequences:

  • Courts often avoid constitutional holdings by narrowly interpreting statutes (Constitutional Avoidance canon)
  • Post-Loper Bright, courts more likely to find statutory limits on agency authority
  • Agencies less likely to receive deference on questions implicating separation of powers

B. Procedural Requirements & Substantive Review

Key Interaction: Procedural adequacy often influences substantive review of agency action.

Major Doctrinal Connections:

  1. Notice-and-Comment + Arbitrary & Capricious Review: Failure to disclose key data or respond to significant comments may render rule arbitrary and capricious (Nova Scotia).
  2. Explanation Requirements + Reasoned Decision-making: Agency must explain reasoning; inadequate explanation leads to substantive invalidity (State Farm).
  3. Vermont Yankee + Hard Look Review: While courts can't impose additional procedures, they can scrutinize outcomes of existing procedures through hard look review.
  4. Policy Change Procedures + Reliance Interests: For policy changes, reliance interests require both procedural consideration and substantive justification (DHS v. Regents).

Practical Consequences:

  • Procedural defects often lead to substantive invalidation
  • Quality of agency record directly affects likelihood of surviving judicial review
  • Courts may use arbitrary & capricious review to enforce de facto procedural requirements

C. Judicial Review Access & Standards of Review

Key Interaction: Threshold questions of reviewability interact with substantive standards of review.

Major Doctrinal Connections:

  1. Final Agency Action + Ripeness: Bennett's "legal consequences" test overlaps with Abbott Labs' hardship analysis in ripeness doctrine. Both ask about practical impact on regulated parties.
  2. Standing + Zone of Interests: Constitutional injury requirement interacts with statutory "zone of interests" test. Both limit who can challenge agency action.
  3. Committed to Agency Discretion + Arbitrary & Capricious: "No law to apply" under ยง 701(a)(2) means no standard for arbitrary & capricious review. If agency has discretion, review becomes more deferential.
  4. Exhaustion + Ripeness: Both doctrines concern timing of review. Exhaustion focuses on administrative remedies; ripeness on factual development and hardship.

Practical Consequences:

  • Courts sometimes blur distinctions between reviewability doctrines
  • Degree of judicial review correlates with specificity of statutory constraints
  • Litigation strategy often involves forum-shopping for favorable threshold doctrine interpretations

D. Agency Authority & Policymaking Methods

Key Interaction: Agency policymaking choices are shaped by constraints on substantive authority.

Major Doctrinal Connections:

  1. Chevron Deference + Rulemaking/Adjudication Choice: Under Chevron, agencies had incentive to use formal procedures to gain deference. Post-Loper Bright, this incentive diminishes.
  2. Major Questions + Notice-and-Comment: Major policy initiatives face both procedural hurdles (notice-and-comment) and substantive hurdles (clear authorization).
  3. Resource Constraints + Enforcement Discretion: Limited resources lead agencies to issue guidance documents and policy statements rather than rules, affecting reviewability.
  4. Political Controls + Procedural Choices: Presidential oversight influences agency choices between rulemaking, adjudication, and guidance.

Practical Consequences:

  • Agencies strategically select policymaking forms based on legal constraints
  • Post-Loper Bright, reduced incentive for formal procedures
  • Increased use of guidance documents may lead to less transparent policymaking

II. Issue-Spotting Guides

A. Agency Action Analysis Template

1. Identify Agency Action

  • What did the agency do? (Rule, order, guidance, denial, inaction)
  • What procedural form did it take? (Formal/informal rulemaking, formal/informal adjudication, etc.)
  • What statutory authority is the agency acting under?

2. Assess Procedural Compliance

  • If rulemaking: Were notice-and-comment procedures followed? Any exemptions apply?
  • If adjudication: Were appropriate hearings provided? Bias concerns?
  • Were any statutory-specific procedures required beyond APA?
  • Was explanation for action adequate?

3. Evaluate Substantive Authority

  • Does agency have statutory authority for this action?
  • Is this a major question requiring clear authorization?
  • Has Congress delegated with an intelligible principle?
  • Post-Loper Bright: Is agency's interpretation the best reading of statute?

4. Check Constitutional Constraints

  • Does agency structure comply with Appointments Clause?
  • Do removal restrictions improperly limit presidential control?
  • Does action violate other constitutional provisions (Due Process, 1st Amendment, etc.)?

5. Apply Standard of Review

  • Is the action arbitrary and capricious?
  • For formal proceedings: Is it supported by substantial evidence?
  • For legal interpretations: Best reading under traditional tools?
  • For policy choices: Reasoned explanation provided?

B. Judicial Review Checklist

1. Threshold Requirements

  • Standing: Injury-in-fact, causation, redressability; special status (state, procedural, associational)?
  • Final Agency Action: Bennett two-part test (consummation + legal consequences)
  • Exhaustion: Administrative remedies pursued? Exceptions apply?
  • Ripeness: Fitness for judicial review? Hardship from delaying review?
  • Reviewability: Precluded by statute? Committed to agency discretion?
  • Zone of Interests: Plaintiff within zone protected by statute?

2. Scope of Review

  • Record: What materials comprise administrative record?
  • Issues: Fact, law, policy, or mixed questions?
  • Relief: Vacatur, remand, injunction, declaratory judgment?
  • Timing: Pre-enforcement or post-enforcement challenge?

3. Standards of Review

  • Statutory Interpretation: De novo, best reading post-Loper Bright
  • Policy Judgments: Arbitrary and capricious, State Farm factors
  • Factual Findings: Substantial evidence (formal), arbitrary and capricious (informal)
  • APA Procedural Requirements: De novo review
  • Constitutional Questions: De novo review
  • Agency Interpretation of Own Rules: Kisor 5-part test

C. Exam-Specific Issue Identification

Rulemaking Process Questions - Look For:

  • Rule type classification problems (legislative vs. interpretive vs. policy)
  • Notice adequacy issues (disclosure of data, methodology, alternatives)
  • Comment response problems (significant comments ignored)
  • Logical outgrowth issues (final rule differs from proposal)
  • Asserted exemptions from notice-and-comment (good cause, etc.)

Statutory Authority Questions - Look For:

  • Ambiguous statutory language
  • Agency acting at edge of authority
  • Major economic or political significance
  • Novel or expansive interpretations of long-standing statutes
  • Conflicts between specific and general statutory provisions

Agency Policy Change Questions - Look For:

  • Reliance interests affected by change
  • Contradictory factual findings
  • Failure to acknowledge change
  • Political motivation for change
  • Alternatives to complete rescission

Judicial Review Questions - Look For:

  • Timing issues (pre-enforcement, ripeness)
  • Standing challenges (generalized grievances, attenuated causation)
  • Interagency actions or multi-stage processes
  • Discretionary enforcement decisions
  • Procedural violations without substantive impact

III. Common Exam Mistakes

A. Analytical Mistakes

  1. Confusing Deference Standards: Applying Chevron post-Loper Bright or using wrong deference framework for agency action type
  2. Applying Wrong APA Section: Confusing rulemaking and adjudication requirements or applying formal procedures to informal actions
  3. Failing to Separate Process from Substance: Not distinguishing between procedural compliance and substantive validity
  4. Misidentifying Rule Types: Confusion between legislative, interpretive, and policy statements
  5. Omitting Threshold Analysis: Jumping to merits without addressing standing, finality, or ripeness

B. Structure and Organization Mistakes

  1. Failing to Use IRAC: Not clearly identifying issues, rules, analysis, and conclusions
  2. Inadequate Rule Statements: Vague statements of law without specific tests or factors
  3. Conclusory Analysis: Stating conclusions without connecting facts to legal standards
  4. Disorganized Presentation: Addressing issues in illogical order or mixing analyses
  5. Unbalanced Analysis: Exploring one side of argument without addressing counterarguments

C. Best Practices

  1. Methodical Analysis: Work through doctrines step-by-step, applying each element or factor
  2. Focus on Tests: Frame analysis around specific legal tests (State Farm, Lujan, etc.)
  3. Connect Facts to Law: Explicitly link case facts to relevant legal standards
  4. Address Both Sides: Present best arguments for different positions
  5. Consider Judicial Remedies: Discuss appropriate relief (vacatur, remand, injunction)

IV. Recent Developments (2020-2024)

A. Statutory Interpretation Revolution

Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024):

  • Overturned Chevron deference after 40 years
  • Courts now determine "best reading" of statutes
  • Agency interpretations evaluated under Skidmore framework
  • Reduces agency flexibility in statutory interpretation
  • Enhances judicial role in agency oversight

Practical Implications:

  • Agencies must develop stronger textual arguments for statutory authority
  • More reliance on other tools to build persuasive cases
  • Increased litigation over statutory interpretation
  • More need for explicit congressional authorization

B. Major Questions Doctrine Formalization

West Virginia v. EPA (2022) & NFIB v. OSHA (2022):

  • Formalized Major Questions Doctrine as distinct inquiry
  • Applied to strike down COVID vaccine mandate and Clean Power Plan
  • Requires clear congressional authorization for questions of vast economic/political significance
  • Especially skeptical of "novel" agency assertions of authority

Practical Implications:

  • Constrains agency ability to address major problems through creative statutory interpretation
  • Creates additional hurdle for ambitious regulatory programs
  • May require more specific congressional legislation
  • Encourages incremental rather than transformative regulation

C. Evolving Presidential Control Doctrine

Seila Law v. CFPB (2020) & Collins v. Yellen (2021):

  • Struck down single-director structure with removal protection
  • Limited Humphrey's Executor to multi-member expert bodies
  • Expanded presidential removal power
  • Distinguished between constitutional structure and agency actions

U.S. v. Arthrex (2021):

  • Addressed Appointments Clause issues for administrative patent judges
  • Found APJs were exercising power reserved for principal officers
  • Remedied by making decisions subject to Director review
  • Demonstrated concern with accountability in agency structure

D. Rigorous Arbitrary & Capricious Review

Department of Homeland Security v. Regents (2020):

  • Invalidated DACA rescission as arbitrary and capricious
  • Required consideration of reliance interests
  • Required consideration of alternatives to full rescission
  • Rejected post hoc rationalizations

Department of Commerce v. New York (2019):

  • Rejected citizenship question on census due to pretextual reasoning
  • Emphasized importance of truthful agency explanations
  • Showed willingness to look beyond administrative record

Practical Implications:

  • Agencies must provide thorough, honest explanations
  • Consideration of reliance interests and alternatives required
  • Courts more willing to scrutinize agency motivations
