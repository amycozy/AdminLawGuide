ADMINISTRATIVE LAW EXAM REFERENCE - DOCTRINE COMPARISONS

Administrative Law Exam Reference - Doctrine Comparisons

How to Use This Section: This part contains comparison tables of key administrative law doctrines. These tables help you identify similarities and differences between related concepts and understand how different legal frameworks interact. Use these for quick reference when analyzing complex administrative law problems.

IV. Pre/Post-Loper Bright Analysis

Aspect Pre-Loper Bright (Chevron Era) Post-Loper Bright (Current)
Key Precedent Chevron v. NRDC (1984) Loper Bright v. Raimondo (2024)
Analytical Framework Two-step process:
1) Is statute ambiguous?
2) If yes, is agency's interpretation reasonable?		 Courts determine "best reading" of statute using traditional tools of interpretation
Role of Courts • Identify statutory ambiguity
• Defer to reasonable agency interpretations
• Not decide which interpretation is best		 • Exercise independent judgment
• Determine best reading of statute
• Consider but not defer to agency views
Role of Agencies • Primary interpreters of ambiguous statutes
• Entitled to deference when statute ambiguous
• Policy choices respected in ambiguous spaces		 • Technical experts offering persuasive views
• No automatic deference
• Must persuade courts of interpretive correctness
Agency Flexibility Greater: Agencies could change interpretations of ambiguous statutes more easily Lesser: Changes must persuade courts they represent best reading, not just reasonable reading
Relevance of Agency Expertise Central justification for deference; presumed for ambiguous statutes Considered for persuasive weight under Skidmore, but not basis for deference
Political Accountability Major justification for Chevron: Agencies more politically accountable than courts Rejected as basis for surrendering judicial interpretive duty
Stability of Interpretations Potentially less stable; agencies could change with administrations Potentially more stable; court precedents more difficult to overturn
Burden on Challengers Heavy: Must show statute unambiguous or agency unreasonable Lighter: Must show agency's reading isn't best interpretation
Relationship to Major Questions Doctrine Exception to Chevron for questions of vast economic/political significance Reinforced by Loper Bright; both limit agency authority in significant cases

V. Standing Variations

Standing Type Requirements Special Considerations Key Cases
Basic Article III Standing 1) Injury in fact
2) Causation
3) Redressability		 • Constitutional minimum
• Concrete, particularized, actual/imminent injury
• Connection between challenged action and injury
• Remedy likely to redress injury		 Lujan v. Defenders (1992)
State Standing Same basic elements, but with "special solicitude" • Quasi-sovereign interests
• Protection of natural resources
• Citizens' health and welfare
• Relaxed causation/redressability
• Parens patriae interests		 Massachusetts v. EPA (2007)
Procedural Standing • Violation of procedural right
• Concrete interest affected		 • Relaxed imminence requirement
• Relaxed redressability requirement
• Still requires concrete stake
• Often environmental/administrative cases		 Lujan (discussion of procedure)
Summers v. Earth Island (2009)
Associational Standing 1) Members would have standing
2) Interests germane to organization's purpose
3) Individual participation not required		 • Organization sues on behalf of members
• Must identify specific affected members
• Must show specific plans or activities affected		 Hunt v. Washington Apple (1977)
Summers (2009)
Competitor Standing • Economic injury from market distortion
• Competitive disadvantage		 • Economic injury presumed sufficient
• Market participant challenging regulation
• Increased competition as injury		 Association of Data Processing (1970)
Taxpayer Standing • Generally rejected except for Establishment Clause challenges • Extremely narrow
• Must challenge specific congressional appropriation
• Only for Establishment Clause
• Generally insufficient		 Flast v. Cohen (1968)
Hein v. Freedom From Religion (2007)
Zone of Interests Test (Prudential) • Plaintiff's interests must fall within zone of interests protected or regulated by statute • Not "especially demanding" test
• Part of prudential standing
• Forecloses suit only when interests "marginally related" to statute		 Lexmark Int'l v. Static Control (2014)

VI. Separation of Powers Doctrines

Doctrine Core Principle Test/Analysis Key Cases
Nondelegation Doctrine Congress cannot delegate legislative power without "intelligible principle" • Statute must provide "intelligible principle" to guide agency discretion
• Extremely deferential historically
• Potential revitalization signaled in Gundy dissent		 Schechter Poultry (1935)
American Trucking (2001)
Gundy (2019)
Bicameralism & Presentment Legislative actions require passage by both houses and presentment to President • Formal requirements for legislation in Article I
• Actions with legislative effect must follow Article I process
• No shortcuts allowed		 INS v. Chadha (1983)
Clinton v. New York (1998)
Appointments Clause President appoints "Officers of the United States"; principal officers require Senate confirmation • Principal officers: Presidential appointment + Senate confirmation
• Inferior officers: Can be appointed by President, courts, or department heads
• Employees: No constitutional appointment requirements		 Buckley v. Valeo (1976)
Edmond v. US (1997)
Lucia v. SEC (2018)
Presidential Removal Power President has constitutional authority to remove executive officers • General rule: President can remove executive officers at will
• Exceptions: (1) Multi-member expert bodies (Humphrey's), (2) Inferior officers with limited duties (Morrison)
• No multiple layers of for-cause protection		 Myers v. US (1926)
Humphrey's Executor (1935)
Morrison v. Olson (1988)
Free Enterprise Fund (2010)
Seila Law (2020)
Take Care Clause President shall "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed" • Basis for presidential control over administration
• Foundation for removal power
• Limits on executive non-enforcement		 Heckler v. Chaney (1985)
United States v. Texas (2023)
Major Questions Doctrine Agencies need clear congressional authorization for decisions of vast economic/political significance • Skepticism of agency claims to discover novel powers in long-extant statutes
• Clear statement rule for major questions
• Courts look for specific congressional authorization for significant agency actions		 Brown & Williamson (2000)
West Virginia v. EPA (2022)
NFIB v. OSHA (2022)
Article III Judicial Power Judicial power vested in courts created under Article III • Courts have power to say what the law is
• Agency adjudications require Article III court review
• Limited delegation of adjudicatory power to agencies		 Marbury v. Madison (1803)
Crowell v. Benson (1932)
Stern v. Marshall (2011)
