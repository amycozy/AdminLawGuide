ADMINISTRATIVE LAW EXAM REFERENCE - CORE LEGAL TESTS

Administrative Law Exam Reference - Core Legal Tests

How to Use This Section: This part contains the essential legal tests you'll need to apply on your exam. Each test is presented in a step-by-step format with key elements highlighted. Memorize these frameworks and be prepared to apply them to fact patterns on your exam.

I. Arbitrary & Capricious Review

A. State Farm Test

Under Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Ass'n v. State Farm (463 U.S. 29, 1983), agency action is arbitrary and capricious if:

  1. The agency relied on factors Congress did not intend it to consider
  2. The agency entirely failed to consider an important aspect of the problem
  3. The agency offered an explanation that runs counter to the evidence
  4. The explanation is so implausible that it cannot be ascribed to a difference in view or agency expertise

This test applies to all informal agency actions, including informal rulemaking, informal adjudication, and policy determinations.

B. Policy Changes

When an agency changes policy, under FCC v. Fox Television Stations (556 U.S. 502, 2009), it must:

  1. Display awareness that it is changing position
  2. Provide good reasons for the new policy
  3. NOT prove that the new policy is better than the old one
  4. Explain why it changed when its old policy was based on factual findings that contradict its new policy

C. Reliance Interests

When rescinding a prior policy that created reliance interests, under DHS v. Regents (140 S.Ct. 1891, 2020), the agency must:

  1. Consider reliance interests created by the prior policy
  2. Assess whether there are less disruptive alternatives to complete rescission
  3. Explain its reasoning adequately

II. Standing Analysis

A. Constitutional Standing (Lujan Test)

From Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife (504 U.S. 555, 1992), standing requires:

  1. Injury in Fact: Concrete, particularized, actual/imminent injury
  2. Causation: Injury must be fairly traceable to the challenged action
  3. Redressability: Likely (not speculative) that the injury will be redressed by a favorable decision

Plaintiff bears the burden of proving standing.

B. Special Standing Variations

1. States: Massachusetts v. EPA (549 U.S. 497, 2007)

  • "Special solicitude" in standing analysis due to quasi-sovereign interests
  • States can assert interests in protecting natural resources and citizens' health/welfare
  • Relaxed causation and redressability requirements

2. Procedural Rights:

  • When a procedural right is violated (e.g., right to EIS under NEPA), plaintiff need not meet normal redressability and immediacy standards
  • Still must show concrete interest affected by agency action

3. Associations:

  • Can sue on behalf of members if: (1) members would have standing; (2) interests germane to organization's purpose; (3) individual participation not required

C. Zone of Interests Test

From Lexmark Int'l, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc. (572 U.S. 118, 2014):

  • Plaintiff must fall within "zone of interests" protected or regulated by the statute
  • Not especially demanding; forecloses suit only when interests are "marginally related" or "inconsistent" with statutory purpose
  • Focus on whether plaintiff is within class of persons Congress intended to benefit

III. Major Questions Doctrine

A. Basic Framework

From West Virginia v. EPA (142 S.Ct. 2587, 2022) and NFIB v. OSHA (142 S.Ct. 661, 2022):

  • When agency claims power to make decisions of vast economic or political significance, courts apply heightened scrutiny
  • Agency must point to clear congressional authorization for such authority

B. Triggering Factors (MARS)

Courts are particularly skeptical when:

  • Major economic or political significance at stake
  • Agency claims newfound authority in long-existing statute
  • Regulatory history shows no previous similar assertions of power
  • Statutory context suggests Congress would have spoken clearly if it intended such delegation

Additional warning signs:

  • Agency regulates in area of traditional state authority
  • Agency creates new regulatory regime with far-reaching impacts
  • Congress has considered but not enacted similar policy
  • Agency acts beyond its area of expertise

IV. Nondelegation Doctrine

A. Intelligible Principle Test

From J.W. Hampton, Jr. & Co. v. United States (276 U.S. 394, 1928) and Whitman v. American Trucking Associations (531 U.S. 457, 2001):

  • Congress must provide an "intelligible principle" to guide agency discretion
  • Statute must set boundaries and provide standards for agency to follow
  • Constitutional challenge will succeed only if Congress provided "no standards" to constrain agency (extremely rare)

Only two successful challenges: Panama Refining Co. v. Ryan (1935) and Schechter Poultry v. United States (1935).

B. Recent Developments

From Gundy v. United States (139 S.Ct. 2116, 2019):

  • Plurality upheld SORNA delegation to Attorney General
  • Gorsuch dissent (joined by Roberts and Thomas) advocated stricter approach
  • Alito concurred in judgment only; Kavanaugh didn't participate
  • Suggests possible revival of stronger nondelegation doctrine in future case

V. Final Agency Action Test

A. Bennett Two-Part Test

From Bennett v. Spear (520 U.S. 154, 1997), agency action is "final" when:

  1. It marks the consummation of the agency's decision-making process (not tentative or interlocutory)
  2. It determines rights or obligations or has legal consequences

Both elements must be satisfied. Finality is required for APA review under ยง 704.

B. Recent Refinements

From Sackett v. EPA (566 U.S. 120, 2012) and U.S. Army Corps of Engineers v. Hawkes Co. (136 S.Ct. 1807, 2016):

  • Agency compliance orders that expose parties to increased penalties are final
  • Jurisdictional determinations with direct and appreciable legal consequences are final
  • Inability to obtain immediate judicial review is a hardship factor supporting finality

VI. Ripeness Analysis

A. Abbott Labs Two-Part Test

From Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner (387 U.S. 136, 1967), courts evaluate:

  1. Fitness for judicial decision: Is the issue purely legal or would it benefit from further factual development?
  2. Hardship to parties: Would withholding review cause significant hardship?

Courts balance these factors when determining ripeness.

B. Pre-enforcement Review

Pre-enforcement review is generally available when:

  • Challenge presents pure legal question
  • Regulation is "final agency action"
  • Regulation has direct and immediate effect on daily business
  • Delaying review would force regulated entity to choose between:
    • Costly compliance with potentially invalid rule, or
    • Risking serious penalties for noncompliance

VII. Statutory Interpretation Post-Loper Bright

A. End of Chevron Deference

From Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (600 U.S. ___, 2024):

  • Overruled Chevron v. NRDC (1984) deference framework
  • Courts must determine "best reading" of statute using traditional tools of interpretation
  • No more deference to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes
  • Agency expertise is still relevant but not controlling

B. Current Interpretive Approach

Courts now apply traditional interpretive tools:

  1. Text: Plain meaning of statutory language
  2. Structure: How provision fits within broader statutory context
  3. Purpose: Objectives Congress sought to achieve
  4. History: Legislative history and historical context
  5. Precedent: Prior judicial interpretations
  6. Canons: Traditional canons of construction

Agency's interpretation may be considered for its persuasiveness under Skidmore v. Swift & Co. (323 U.S. 134, 1944), based on:

  • Thoroughness of agency's consideration
  • Validity of its reasoning
  • Consistency with earlier and later pronouncements
  • Agency's expertise in the area
